Financial Institutions

Who moves money through the economy — and why they exist at all.

01
Week 01 · Semester 1

The Financial System

The big idea A financial system exists to move money from people who have it but no use for it, to people who have a use but no money.

Imagine you saved £10,000 over the summer, while someone across town wants to start a food-truck business but has nothing. Without a financial system, your cash sits "under the mattress" earning nothing, and their good idea never happens. The financial system connects the two — and takes a small cut for doing it.

Savers lend, spenders borrow

Funds flow from lender-savers to borrower-spenders. The same four groups appear on both sides — households, firms, government and foreigners — because anyone can be in surplus or deficit at different times.

Two routes for the money

  • Direct finance — borrowers raise funds straight from lenders in markets (issuing shares or bonds).
  • Indirect finance — a financial intermediary (a bank) sits in the middle. In every major economy this is far bigger than direct finance.

How markets are sliced

  • Debt vs equity — debt pays interest (bonds, loans); equity pays dividends and means ownership (shares).
  • Primary vs secondary — primary = brand-new securities sold to first buyers (often via an investment bank); secondary = reselling them afterwards (e.g. the London Stock Exchange).
  • Money vs capital — money market = short-term (< 1 year); capital market = long-term (> 1 year) plus equities (no maturity).

The cast of intermediaries

Depository institutions (banks) take deposits and make loans — commercial banks and building societies. Non-depository institutions split into contractual savings (insurance companies, pension funds) and investment companies (mutual funds / unit trusts, hedge funds, investment banks). You'll meet each of them in the weeks ahead.

🌍 In Plain Life

A bank is a matchmaker for money. On its own a single £100 saver can't afford to research safe investments, and a single borrower can't find them. The bank pools thousands of savers, lends in bulk, and pays you interest for the privilege — better than the mattress.

🔑 Lock It In

"Debt = interest, Equity = ownership." And for the market splits, remember the three knives: what it is (debt/equity), when it's sold (primary/secondary), how long it lasts (money/capital).

💡 Did You Know?

This module is a tale of two halves. Semester 1 follows the intermediaries (the banks and funds). Semester 2 follows the markets they trade in — rates, bonds, currencies and shares. Keep the map in mind and everything connects.

The London Stock Exchange, founded in 1801, is one of the world's oldest and handles over £2 trillion in turnover annually. Globally, the financial system channels roughly $100 trillion in assets through intermediaries every year.

Week 02 · Semester 1

Why Financial Institutions Exist

The big idea Markets struggle when one side knows more than the other. Financial institutions exist mainly to close that information gap.

If markets were perfect, firms would just sell shares and bonds directly and banks would be pointless. Yet across the US, Germany, Japan and Canada the same pattern holds: businesses are financed mostly by indirect finance through banks — not by issuing stocks. Why? Because of two market frictions.

1 · Transaction costs

Researching the "best, safest" stock isn't worth it if you only have £100 to invest. Intermediaries slash these costs through expertise and economies of scale, and provide liquidity services (an account that lets you pay bills and earn interest).

2 · Asymmetric information

One party knows more than the other. It bites in two ways:

  • Adverse selection — happens before the deal. The people most eager for a loan are often the riskiest.
  • Moral hazard — happens after the deal. Once they have your money, borrowers may take risks they wouldn't with their own.

The Lemons Problem

If buyers can't tell good used cars from "lemons", they only offer the average price — so owners of good cars won't sell, and the market fills with junk. The same logic means good firms won't issue undervalued securities, which is exactly why stock issuance is not the main funding source (and why well-known firms, with less hidden information, find it easier). Fixes: government disclosure rules, intermediaries (the "trusted used-car dealer"), collateral and net worth.

The Principal–Agent Problem

When owners (principals) are separate from managers (agents), managers may serve themselves. Debt contracts, monitoring and covenants help — which is part of why debt is used more than equity.

🏛 Real Story

You put up £9,000 (90%) to open an ice-cream shop; Steve puts £1,000 and runs it. If it earns £50,000 you get £45,000 — but Steve, who keeps only £5,000, spends afternoons at the beach and buys "office art" with the profits. That's moral hazard in an equity contract: how do you make Steve work hard for money that's mostly yours?

🔑 Lock It In

Adverse selection = hidden type, before. Moral hazard = hidden action, after. "Select the wrong type before you sign; they behave badly after."

💡 Did You Know?

These two gremlins return again and again — they're the engine behind financial crises and the whole business model of insurance.

George Akerlof's 1970 paper on the "lemons problem" won him the Nobel Prize in Economics in 2001. In the US used-car market, estimates suggest lemons make up roughly 1 in 4 private sales — exactly the kind of information gap that collapses markets.

Week 03 · Semester 1

Commercial Banks & Money Creation

The big idea Banks don't simply lend out the deposits savers bring them — they create brand-new money every time they make a loan.

This is the week that breaks most people's intuition. We meet the Bank of England, the different kinds of "money", and the surprising mechanics of how money is born.

Forms of money

  • Cash (notes & coins) — issued by the Bank of England, but only ~3% of the money supply.
  • Electronic money — central-bank reserves held by banks at the BoE, and the deposits banks create for customers when they lend.

Measuring the money supply by liquidity

Narrow money (M0) = cash in circulation + reserves at the BoE. Broad money climbs from there: M1 = cash + current accounts; M2 adds savings; M4 adds other deposits at UK banks and building societies. The deeper you go, the less liquid the assets.

The money multiplier

A bank keeps a fraction of a deposit and lends the rest. That loan gets spent and re-deposited at another bank, which keeps a fraction and lends again… and the money supply balloons. If Bank A lends £9m, it lands at Bank B as a £9m deposit, which lends £8.1m to the next borrower, and so on.

⊞ The Reckoner · Money Multiplier
🔑 Lock It In

"Loans create deposits." The textbook multiplier is a simplified picture — note the UK has no reserve-requirement ratio, so banks aren't mechanically constrained by reserves.

💡 Did You Know?

Roughly 97% of the money in the economy isn't cash at all — it's electronic deposits created by commercial banks when they lend. The notes in your wallet are the tiny tip of the iceberg.

During the 2008 crisis, the Bank of England created £435 billion through quantitative easing — buying government bonds to inject money directly. By 2020, that total had risen to £895 billion, demonstrating that central banks create money too, but through a different channel.

🌍 In Plain Life

Two myths to bust: (1) banks are not just middlemen handing over savers' cash — they conjure new deposits; (2) the central bank doesn't fix the quantity of money so much as the price of it — the interest rate.

Week 04 · Semester 1

The UK Banking Sector

The big idea UK retail banking is dominated by a handful of giants — and structural forces make that concentration stubbornly hard to break.

Retail banking here is ruled by the "Big Five": HSBC, Barclays, Lloyds, NatWest and Santander. To understand why, we measure concentration and then ask what keeps the small players small.

Building societies

Born in Birmingham in 1775 as mutual organisations owned by their members (like credit unions). After 1980s "demutualisation" many became shareholder banks; the largest survivor, Nationwide, still holds 80%+ of building-society assets and remains a mutual.

Measuring concentration: the HHI

HHI = Σ (market share of each firm)²
< 1,000 un-concentrated · 1,000–2,000 moderate · > 2,000 highly concentrated · 10,000 = pure monopoly
⊞ The Reckoner · Market Concentration (HHI)

Why concentration persists

  • Scale & incumbency — big banks spread fixed costs (IT, HR) thinner, hold more diverse, lower-risk portfolios, and enjoy customer trust.
  • Regulation favours the big — large banks meet capital requirements more easily; accreditation is slow and resource-heavy.
  • Customer disengagement — inertia is huge. People rarely switch banks even for better rates, and bundled, complex products make comparison hard.

Challenger banks (Starling, Monzo, Atom, Metro) and Open Banking — sharing your data with third-party apps, with consent — are the policy attempts to inject competition.

🌍 In Plain Life

Why have you probably never switched banks? Switching feels like effort for a tiny gain — that's customer inertia. Energy bills got price-comparison sites; banking mostly didn't, so the giants keep their grip.

💡 Did You Know?

Square every firm's market share and add them up: an HHI of 10,000 means one firm owns 100% of the market — a perfect monopoly. Regulators reach for the HHI whenever a merger might lessen competition.

The UK personal current account market has an HHI of roughly 1,800–2,000 — right on the border of "highly concentrated." After the 2008 crisis, the UK government forced Lloyds to sell off TSB to reduce concentration, yet the Big Five still hold roughly 85% of all UK current accounts.

Week 05 · Semester 1

Banking Systems: USA & China

The big idea Countries organise banking very differently — keep commercial and investment banking apart, fuse them into one "universal" bank, or run the whole thing through the state.

Two classic models

  • Universal banking (German model) — one bank does everything and even holds big stakes in industrial firms (Deutsche Bank once part-owned Daimler-Benz, Siemens). One-stop shopping, but complex risk and conflicts of interest.
  • Market-based (US model) — historically a clear wall between commercial banks (Citibank) and investment banks (Morgan Stanley). Cleaner risk separation, but higher transaction costs.

The US wall, and its fall

The Glass-Steagall Act (1933) separated commercial and investment banking after the Great Depression. By the 1990s big banks were dodging it; it was repealed in 1999, fuelling the rise of huge "bank holding companies". The Riegle-Neal Act (1994) had already allowed full interstate branching, driving decades of mergers.

Safety nets differ

The US created the first national deposit insurance (the FDIC, 1933) — today covering deposits up to $250,000. The UK's scheme came after the 1980s and covers up to £85,000. Reserve requirements? The US ratio is 0% (since March 2020); the UK has none, relying on capital requirements instead.

The Chinese system

State-led from 1949. The "Big Four" are state-owned and hold ~50% of the market. Bank loans reached about 128% of GDP (vs ~48% for the US), funnelled toward state enterprises and infrastructure — fuelling growth, but leaving small firms reliant on shadow banking and saddling big banks with non-performing loans.

🔑 Lock It In

Universal = "everything under one roof" (Germany). Market-based = "walls between rooms" (US). Glass-Steagall built the wall in 1933 and knocked it down in 1999.

🌍 In Plain Life

Deposit insurance is a comfort blanket — but it can breed moral hazard: if your savings are guaranteed, you won't care whether your bank makes reckless loans. Protection quietly removes the incentive to be careful.

💡 Did You Know?

By 2016, China's banking assets were larger than the entire euro area's banking systems put together. A system that barely existed in 1949 became the world's biggest in a single lifetime.

China's shadow banking sector — wealth management products, trust loans, and peer-to-peer lending — peaked at roughly $10 trillion in 2017, nearly 80% of GDP. The FDIC's Deposit Insurance Fund holds roughly $120 billion to cover $9 trillion in insured deposits — a coverage ratio of just 1.3%.

Week 06 · Semester 1

Investment Banks

The big idea Investment banks take no deposits. They help companies raise capital and broker deals — and get paid handsomely in fees.

Originally "merchant banks" in the UK, these non-depository institutions are the dealmakers of finance. JP Morgan (founded 1871) once organised a syndicate to rescue the US Treasury's gold and helped halt the Panic of 1907. Goldman Sachs (1869) ran one of the first IPOs (Sears, 1906) and stayed a partnership until 1999.

What they actually do

  • Underwriting stocks and bonds for companies going public or issuing debt.
  • Market making — quoting buy/sell prices to keep a security liquid.
  • Equity research and brokerage for institutional clients.
  • Mergers & acquisitions advice (including hostile takeovers).

Three ways to underwrite

  • Firm commitment — the bank buys the whole issue at a fixed price and resells it (it carries the risk; often shares it via a syndicate).
  • Best efforts — the bank just tries its best to sell; doesn't buy the issue.
  • Private placement — sold to a small select group; lighter regulation (common for bonds).

The industry is tiered into bulge bracket (Goldman, JPMorgan, Morgan Stanley…), middle market, and boutiques. Typical fees: M&A ~1–2% of deal value; IPO underwriting ~4–7%. The career ladder runs Analyst → Associate → VP → Director → Managing Director.

🏛 Real Story

In 1995 a single Singapore-based trader, Nick Leeson, lost £827 million on futures speculation and brought down Barings — Britain's oldest merchant bank. After that, American and European banks came to dominate UK investment banking.

💡 Did You Know?

Goldman Sachs is nicknamed "Government Sachs": alumni Robert Rubin, Henry Paulson and Steven Mnuchin all went on to become US Secretary of the Treasury.

Global investment banking fees exceeded $120 billion in 2021 — a record year for M&A and IPO activity. The top 10 banks alone captured roughly 55% of all fee revenue, making this one of the most concentrated industries on earth.

🔑 Lock It In

Underwriting = "Firm buys, Best tries, Private hides." Firm commitment buys the lot; best efforts only tries; private placement hides it with a select few.

Week 07 · Semester 1

Islamic Banking & Finance

The big idea A financial system built on sharing real risk in real assets — not on charging interest.

Islamic finance provides services in line with Shari'ah law. Rather than lending money to earn interest, it ties returns to genuine economic activity and shared risk.

Three core prohibitions

  • Riba (interest) — earning a guaranteed return on money alone, without effort or risk, is forbidden as unjust gain.
  • Gharar (excessive uncertainty) — contracts with unclear terms or outcomes are out (you can't sell "a fish still in the sea").
  • Maisir (gambling) — pure games of chance where one wins only because others lose.

Instead, finance must use profit-and-loss sharing, be asset-backed, and stay ethical (no alcohol, tobacco, gambling, weapons, conventional banking).

The key products

  • Murabaha — cost-plus: the bank buys an asset and resells it to you at an agreed markup (no interest, just transparent profit).
  • Ijara — leasing: the bank owns the asset and rents it to you.
  • Musharaka — partnership: both put in capital and share profits and losses.
  • Sukuk — "Islamic bonds": certificates of partial ownership in a real asset, paying returns from its performance rather than interest.

Islamic social finance adds Zakat (compulsory alms), Sadaqat (voluntary charity), Waqf (endowment) and Qard Hassan (a benevolent, interest-free loan).

🔑 Lock It In

The three "no"s: Riba (no interest), Gharar (no fog), Maisir (no gambling). Replace them with sharing real risk in real assets.

💡 Did You Know?

Islamic financial assets exploded from $386 billion in 2006 to about $4.5 trillion in 2023, serving ~150 million customers. The UK is the main Western hub — and over 191 conventional banks (HSBC, Citi, UBS…) now run Islamic "windows".

The UK has issued more sukuk (Islamic bonds) than any other Western country — including a landmark £200 million sovereign sukuk in 2014. Malaysia and Saudi Arabia remain the largest markets, holding over 60% of global Islamic banking assets between them.

Week 08 · Semester 1

Financial Crises I — Securitization & 2008

The big idea Repackaging illiquid loans into tradable securities let banks pass on their risk — and quietly wired the whole system to a single point of failure: US house prices.

From "hold" to "distribute"

Traditional banking was originate-and-hold: a bank made a 25-year mortgage and kept it. That creates a maturity mismatch — long, illiquid assets funded by short, liquid deposits. Securitization flipped this to originate-to-distribute: convert loans into securities and sell the risk on.

The chain

  1. An originator bank makes the loans.
  2. They're sold to a Special Purpose Vehicle (SPV), separate from the bank.
  3. The SPV issues new securities backed by them — MBS (mortgages) or ABS (other loans) — sliced into tranches by risk.
  4. A credit rating agency rates them; an investment bank underwrites and sells them. Investors even re-pooled tranches into CDOs, and bought credit default swaps (CDS) as insurance.

How it broke

Banks lent to sub-prime borrowers (average credit score 624 vs 742 for prime) using low "teaser" rates. When US rates rose and teasers expired, defaults surged, house prices fell, and the MBS/CDOs underneath turned out to be nearly worthless. Banks stopped lending to each other — the credit crunch.

🏛 Real Story

Goldman Sachs marketed risky CDOs to clients while simultaneously betting against them using credit default swaps. When the market collapsed, Goldman made millions from those bets — profiting by wagering against its own products and customers. Citigroup, Merrill Lynch and JP Morgan later paid hundreds of millions to settle misleading-investor charges.

💡 Did You Know?

By 2007 the pool of "triple-A" (supposedly safest) securities held more than $3 trillion of sub-prime loans. After the crisis they were downgraded to "junk" — exposing a deep conflict of interest: rating agencies are paid by the very issuers they grade.

The global MBS market grew from roughly $2 trillion in 2000 to over $7 trillion by 2007. The "Big Three" rating agencies (S&P, Moody's, Fitch) controlled roughly 95% of the market and earned premium fees for each structured product they rated — creating a powerful incentive to award top grades.

🌍 In Plain Life

Securitization is pass-the-parcel. Each player takes a fee and hands the risky package on, assuming someone else will be holding it when the music stops. In 2008 the music stopped for everyone at once.

Week 09 · Semester 1

Financial Crises II — Stages & the Great Depression

The big idea A financial crisis is what happens when information flows break down so badly that even healthy borrowers can't get funded — and the economy seizes up.

Remember adverse selection and moral hazard? A crisis is those problems on steroids. Banks are experts at telling good borrowers from bad and monitoring them; when that machinery jams, lending freezes for everyone.

Three stages of a crisis

  1. Initial phase — a credit boom and bust (lending standards slip, then losses force deleveraging) and/or an asset-price bubble that bursts. A spike in uncertainty — often a big firm failing — knocks over the first domino.
  2. Banking crisis — frightened depositors stage bank runs; banks dump assets at fire-sale prices, get sicker, and stop lending.
  3. Debt deflation — prices fall but debts stay fixed, so the real burden of debt rises (a £180k mortgage on a house now worth £150k), deepening and prolonging the slump.

Case study: the Great Depression

Stocks doubled in 1928–29; the Fed tightened to curb speculation; forced selling triggered the 1929 crash. Then a 1930 drought hit farm incomes and banks. Between 1930 and 1933 one-third of US banks failed while the Fed "sat idly" for over two years. Credit spreads — the extra interest nervous lenders demand — jumped from 2% to nearly 8%.

💡 Did You Know?

The Depression saw prices fall ~25% and unemployment hit ~25%. The misery spread worldwide and the resulting discontent helped fuel the rise of fascism and the road to WWII. A financial crisis reshaped the 20th century.

During the COVID-19 panic of March 2020, investors withdrew over $100 billion from US money-market funds in a single week — the modern equivalent of a bank run. Only massive Federal Reserve intervention, including buying corporate bonds for the first time in history, prevented a repeat of 1930s-style contagion.

🌍 In Plain Life

A bank run is self-fulfilling: if you suspect some banks are sick but don't know which, the safe move is to pull your money from all of them — which then sinks even the healthy ones. Fear becomes the fact.

🔑 Lock It In

Boom → Run → Deflation. Stage 1 sets up the dominoes (boom & bust + uncertainty), Stage 2 is the bank run, Stage 3 is debt deflation grinding it out.

Week 10 · Semester 1

Pension Funds

The big idea A pension just shifts money from your working years to your retired years. The real question is: who carries the investment risk?

A pension plan is an asset pool that builds up while you work and pays out when you stop. The biggest distinction is how the benefit is defined.

DB vs DC — who bears the risk

  • Defined Benefit (DB) — a guaranteed income based on salary and years of service. The employer bears the investment risk (and any shortfall).
  • Defined Contribution (DC) — a set amount is paid in; your payout depends on how the investments perform. The employee bears the risk.
DB annual pension = 2% × (avg of final 3 years' salary) × years of service
A typical defined-benefit formula — predictable for the worker, expensive and risky for the employer.

Funding & the UK's three pillars

Schemes are either funded (you build your own pot) or PAYG / unfunded (today's workers pay today's pensioners). UK retirement rests on three pillars: the State Pension (PAYG, max ~£203.85/week, needs 35 years of NI contributions), the workplace pension (you + employer + tax relief), and an individual pension. The self-employed use SIPPs, Stakeholder or SSAS plans.

🏛 Real Story

Carillion's 2018 collapse left 27,000 members of its 13 DB schemes facing a £2.6 billion deficit. With people living longer and interest rates low, many DB funds fell into deficit — which is exactly why employers have rushed to close them and switch staff to DC plans (DC funds can't fall into deficit because nothing is promised).

🔑 Lock It In

DB = Boss's Burden. DC = Counts on you. In DB the employer carries the risk; in DC the employee does.

💡 Did You Know?

The full UK State Pension is about £10,610 a year. The US equivalent (Social Security) can reach roughly £34,800. Ask yourself the lecture's blunt question: is that enough to live on?

Total UK pension fund assets exceed £3 trillion — one of the largest pools of institutional capital in the world. Since 2012, auto-enrolment has brought over 10 million new workers into workplace pensions, though the default contribution rate of 8% (combined employer + employee) is widely seen as too low for a comfortable retirement.

Week 11 · Semester 1

Insurance Companies

The big idea Insurance swaps the terror of a big, uncertain loss for a small, certain premium — by pooling thousands of risks together.

Insurers are non-depository, contractual-savings institutions (like pension funds). You pay a premium; they pool it with everyone else's and pay out the few who suffer a loss. Because they insure millions, the law of large numbers makes their predictions accurate enough to price for profit.

The same two gremlins

Insurance is riddled with asymmetric information:

  • Adverse selection — the people most likely to claim are most likely to buy. (Fix: medical screening, excluding pre-existing conditions.)
  • Moral hazard — once covered, people take less care. (Fix: the tools below.)

The toolkit against moral hazard

  • Risk-based premiums — young drivers pay more.
  • Restrictive provisions — e.g. life cover excludes suicide.
  • Deductibles — you cover the first slice (a £250 deductible on a £1,000 claim → they pay £750).
  • Coinsurance — you share a % (medical plan pays 80%, you pay 20%).
  • Limits & cancellation — caps on payout, and the threat to cancel risky policyholders.
⊞ The Reckoner · Deductible & Coinsurance

Life insurance comes as term (pure cover, no cash value), whole (cover + a savings value you can borrow against) and annuities (insurance against living too long).

🏛 Real Story

AIG thought selling credit default swaps was just more insurance. Its London unit insured over $400 billion of CDS — but unlike banks, AIG only ever sold protection, never hedged by buying it. When mortgage bonds defaulted, AIG had to pay out with nobody paying it. The Fed organised a bailout and took a huge stake. The CDS market had ballooned to about $62 trillion by 2008.

🔑 Lock It In

Deductible = a fixed first slice you pay. Coinsurance = a percentage you share. Both make you act like it's partly your own money — which it is.

💡 Did You Know?

The global insurance industry is worth over $6 trillion in premiums annually. Lloyd's of London, founded in 1686, is not an insurer but a marketplace where syndicates underwrite risks — from ships to satellites to celebrity body parts.

In the UK, the average car insurance premium for a 17-year-old exceeds £1,800 — a stark example of risk-based pricing in action. Meanwhile, the "protection gap" (the difference between insured and uninsured losses) in emerging markets is estimated at over $160 billion annually.

Week 12 · Semester 1

Investment Funds

The big idea Pool your money with thousands of strangers and you instantly get diversification and professional management you could never afford alone.

You can invest directly (buy Tesco shares yourself) or indirectly (buy a stake in a fund that holds many assets). Funds pool everyone's money toward a shared objective, which spreads commission costs and unlocks markets a small investor couldn't reach.

Active vs passive

  • Active management — managers pick winners and dump losers, hoping to beat the market. More expensive (you pay their fees and bonuses).
  • Passive / index trackers — simply mirror an index like the FTSE-100 (if Vodafone is 5% of the index, the fund holds 5% Vodafone). Cheaper, because they buy-and-hold rather than trade.

Open-ended vs closed-ended

Two fund structures at a glance

Funds go by region: UCITS (Europe, often Luxembourg), unit/investment trusts (UK), mutual funds (US). Fees come as front-load, deferred-load or no-load. ETFs are index funds that trade on an exchange in real time, unlike index mutual funds priced once a day at NAV.

🌍 In Plain Life

With £3,000, buying 30 different shares directly means tiny £100 holdings eaten alive by 30 lots of commission. Put the £3,000 in a fund holding 80 companies and the pooled commission is a rounding error — instant diversification for the price of one trade.

🔑 Lock It In

Open-ended grows and shrinks (priced at NAV); closed-ended is fixed and trades like a share (price ≠ NAV). ETF = a tracker you can trade all day long.

💡 Did You Know?

Over the past 15 years, roughly 85% of active US equity fund managers have underperformed the S&P 500 index. That's why passive funds now hold over $20 trillion globally — and the ETF market alone has grown from $1 trillion in 2010 to over $11 trillion today.

The first index fund was created by John Bogle at Vanguard in 1976 with just $11 million in assets. Today, Vanguard manages over $8 trillion — making it one of the largest shareholders of nearly every major publicly traded company on earth.

Financial Markets

The places where money is priced: rates, bonds, currencies and shares.

01
Week 01 · Semester 2

Alternative Investment Funds

The big idea For the wealthy and the bold: funds that use leverage, short-selling and private deals to chase outsized returns — and charge handsomely for trying.

Hedge funds vs ordinary funds

A hedge fund is an investment fund with the brakes off: high minimums (around $1 million), lock-up periods, aggressive use of leverage and derivatives, light regulation, and the famous "2 and 20" fee — ~2% of assets each year plus 20% of profits.

The magic (and danger) of leverage

Leverage means investing with borrowed money to multiply returns:

£100 of yours + £900 borrowed @ 8% → invest £1,000, earn 10% = £1,100
Repay £900 + £72 interest → keep £128. Return on your £100 = 28%. (But losses multiply just as fast.)
⊞ The Reckoner · Leverage Magnifier

Common strategies: equity long-short, market-neutral, short-selling, arbitrage (convertible, fixed-income), global macro, and event-driven.

Private equity

Investing in companies off the public market. Two main types: venture capital (funding start-ups) and leveraged buyouts (taking a public company private). VCs tame asymmetric information by taking board seats, releasing funds in stages, and diversifying across many bets. The deal life-cycle: fundraise → invest (seed → early → late stage) → exit via IPO or sale.

💡 Did You Know?

The top hedge-fund manager of 2023, Chris Hohn (TCI), earned $2.9 billion — in a single year. The top ten together pocketed well over $15 billion. "2 and 20" adds up.

Private equity "dry powder" (committed but unspent capital) reached a record $2.5 trillion globally in 2023 — enough to buy the entire FTSE 100 twice over. Yet studies show the average PE fund barely beats the S&P 500 after fees, raising questions about whether the illiquidity premium is worth the lock-up.

🏛 Real Story

Apple, Microsoft, Starbucks, Facebook, WhatsApp and Snapchat were all venture-backed. VC is one of the very few risky bets pension funds are allowed to make — and when it works, it builds the companies that reshape daily life.

🔑 Lock It In

"2 and 20" = 2% to show up, 20% to perform. And leverage is a magnifying glass — it enlarges gains and losses on your own capital.

Week 02 · Semester 2

Interest Rates

The big idea An interest rate is simply the price of money across time — and almost every other price in finance bends to it.

An interest rate is the "rental price for money": a penalty for spending before you've earned, and a reward for postponing consumption. Like any price, it allocates resources.

Present value — why a pound today beats a pound tomorrow

A pound today can be invested to grow, so future money is worth less now. We discount it back:

PV = CF ÷ (1 + i)n
PV = present value · CF = future cash flow · i = interest rate · n = years
⊞ The Reckoner · Present Value

Why rates exist, and the Fisher Effect

Lenders aren't altruistic: they charge for time preference, for expected inflation, and for risk. Expected inflation is baked into the nominal rate:

i = r + πe
nominal rate = real rate + expected inflation. The real rate is the true signal for borrowing and lending.

What sets the general rate?

The loanable funds model: the rate moves to equate the supply of funds (savings, surpluses, central-bank action) with the demand (consumer credit, business investment, government deficits). Specific rates then vary by creditworthiness, duration, security and lenders' costs. Plotting rate against maturity gives the yield curve — usually upward-sloping, but it can be inverted, humped or flat depending on what lenders expect.

⊞ The Reckoner · Yield-Curve Shapes
🔑 Lock It In

Fisher: nominal = real + expected inflation. If you want the real rate, strip the inflation out of the headline rate.

💡 Did You Know?

An inverted yield curve — where short-term rates exceed long-term ones — has preceded almost every modern US recession. The curve is the market quietly betting that rates (and growth) will fall.

In 2023, the US Treasury yield curve inverted to its deepest level since 1981 — with 2-year yields exceeding 10-year yields by over 1 percentage point. Meanwhile, the European Central Bank pushed rates into negative territory between 2014 and 2022, meaning depositors effectively paid banks to hold their money — a once-unthinkable experiment.

🌍 In Plain Life

Would you rather have £100 now or £100 next year? Now — because you could bank it and have £110. That gut feeling is present value; the interest rate just puts a number on your impatience.

Week 03 · Semester 2

Central Banks

The big idea The bankers' bank and the government's bank — and history shows it works best when politicians can't tell it what to do.

Central banking is old: the Swedish Riksbank (1668), the Bank of England (1694), the US Federal Reserve (1913). Their job grew into guarding monetary stability, financing the state, and acting as lender of last resort to banks in trouble.

What the Bank of England does

Sets monetary policy, safeguards financial stability, issues banknotes, regulates the financial sector, serves as banker to government and to commercial banks, and manages the FX reserves. Three committees run the show: the MPC (sets Bank Rate monthly to hit the inflation target — if it misses, the Governor must write to the Chancellor), the FPC (system-wide stability and stress tests), and the PRC (supervising individual firms). The US parallels are the 12 Reserve Banks, the Board of Governors and the FOMC; the eurozone's is the ECB (1999) with its Governing Council.

Independence: instrument vs goal

  • Instrument independence — freedom to choose how (which tools) to hit the target.
  • Goal independence — freedom to choose what the targets are.

Why independence matters: governments face the political business cycle temptation — juice the economy with low rates before an election, pay for it with inflation afterwards. An independent central bank has no such incentive, and the evidence links independence to lower inflation without hurting growth.

🏛 Real Story

The debate is as old as central banking. Of the freshly created Bank of France in 1806, Napoleon mused that he wanted it "sufficiently in the hands of the government, but not too much." Two centuries on, with euro-area inflation hitting ~10%, that balance is once again a front-line policy fight.

🌍 In Plain Life

An independent central bank is the referee, not a player. If the referee answered to one team's manager, you'd never trust the score. Keep rate-setting away from the people facing re-election and the currency stays credible.

🔑 Lock It In

Instrument = the "how", Goal = the "what". Most modern central banks get instrument independence but are handed their inflation goal by government.

💡 Did You Know?

The Bank of England holds roughly 400,000 bars of gold in its vaults — worth over £200 billion — making it one of the largest custodians of gold on earth. Since 2009, the Federal Reserve's balance sheet expanded from under $1 trillion to over $8 trillion through quantitative easing, fundamentally altering how central banks interact with markets.

Week 04 · Semester 2

Risk Management

The big idea A bank is a juggling act between five kinds of risk — and the deadliest trick is balancing profit against safety.

Banks are vital and fragile, so identifying and managing risk is everything. There are five headline types.

The five risks

  1. Credit risk — borrowers don't repay. Managed by screening, long-term relationships, restrictive covenants, collateral and credit rationing.
  2. Solvency risk — liabilities exceed assets; capital is the cushion that absorbs losses.
  3. Liquidity risk — not enough cash to meet withdrawals, forcing fire-sales.
  4. Interest-rate risk — profits swing when rates move.
  5. Operational & reputation risk — losses from failed processes, people, systems, fraud or scandal.

Solvency — capital as a shock absorber

Two banks each lose £5m on a bad loan. Watch what capital does:

Same £5m loss · capital is the difference between survival and failure

Capital before£10m
Loss−£5m
Capital after£5m ✓
Thinly-capitalised bank
Capital before£4m
Loss−£5m

⊞ The Reckoner · Solvency Cushion

Interest-rate risk: basic gap analysis

Δ profit = (rate-sensitive assets − rate-sensitive liabilities) × Δi
If more of your liabilities reprice than your assets, rising rates squeeze profit.
⊞ The Reckoner · Interest-Rate Gap
💡 Did You Know?

From 2011 to 2016, major banks lost nearly $210 billion to operational risk alone — fines for mis-selling, LIBOR-fixing, FX misconduct and more. As long as people, systems and processes are imperfect, it can never be fully eliminated.

Basel III, the global banking regulation standard, took over a decade to implement after the 2008 crisis. Its Tier 1 capital requirement of 6% (with a 2.5% conservation buffer) means banks must hold roughly 8.5% of risk-weighted assets as loss-absorbing capital — up from just 4% under Basel II. The largest banks face additional "too big to fail" surcharges of up to 3.5%.

🔑 Lock It In

Five risks: "Can Smart Lenders Ignore Operations?" — Credit, Solvency, Liquidity, Interest-rate, Operational. And the eternal trilemma: shareholders want profit, depositors and regulators want safety.

🌍 In Plain Life

Bank capital is the airbag. The riskier the road (the assets), the bigger the airbag you need. But a giant airbag is costly — hold too much capital and your return on equity falls. That trade-off is the heart of bank regulation.

Week 05 · Semester 2

Money Markets

The big idea Where corporate giants park spare cash for a few days — huge, ultra-safe, short-term, and the hidden plumbing of the whole system.

At the end of 2021 Apple reported $64 billion in short-term securities and cash equivalents. It doesn't keep that in a local bank account — so where? The money market: a market for liquidity itself.

What makes it the money market

Instruments are issued in large denominations ($1m+), with short maturities (a day to a year, often < 3 months), low default risk and high liquidity. Central banks use it for open-market operations, so it's also a barometer of conditions.

The instruments

  • Treasury bills — government IOUs sold at a discount, lowest risk.
  • Commercial paper — short-term, unsecured promissory notes from the most creditworthy firms (GE, Citi); avg ~40 days; under 270 days so no SEC registration.
  • Repos — sell a security for cash now with a promise to buy it back slightly dearer in 1–14 days; a workhorse of bank funding and central-bank policy.
  • Certificates of deposit (CDs) — time deposits, negotiable ones trade in a secondary market.
  • Interbank market — banks lend each other money overnight; its rate, LIBOR, long anchored loans worldwide (the US version is the Fed Funds market).
🌍 In Plain Life

A repo is a pawnshop for institutions. You hand over a bond as security, take cash for a few days, then buy the bond back for a touch more — the "touch more" is really just short-term interest. Banks do this constantly to bridge cash gaps.

💡 Did You Know?

When banks refused to lend long-term during the 2008 credit crunch, companies stampeded into commercial paper for short-term cash — and issuance spiked. The same happened at the start of the pandemic. The money market is where stress shows up first.

The US repo market alone handles roughly $1 trillion in daily volume — more than the entire GDP of Switzerland every single day. LIBOR, once the world's most important number, was phased out after the rate-fixing scandal and replaced by risk-free rates like SONIA (UK) and SOFR (US).

🔑 Lock It In

Money-market menu: T-bills, Commercial Paper, Repos, CDs, Interbank. All share the DNA: big, brief, safe, liquid.

Week 06 · Semester 2

The Bond Market

The big idea A bond is a loan you can trade. Master one golden rule and you've got it: price and yield move in opposite directions.

Bonds finance both governments and companies, and government yields act as the reference point that keeps borrowing honest. Gilts (UK) and Treasuries (US) carry virtually no default risk — the issuer can print money — so they're treated as the "risk-free rate" (inflation risk aside). Corporate bonds range from low-risk AAA down to BBB; anything below is "sub-investment grade" (junk).

The vocabulary

  • Coupon rate — annual interest as a % of face value.
  • Face / par value — what you're repaid at maturity.
  • Current yield — coupon ÷ current price.
  • Yield to maturity (YTM) — total return if held to the end, including any capital gain or loss.
current yield ic = C ÷ P
e.g. a £15 coupon on a bond priced at £921 → 15 ÷ 921 = 1.63%

Why price and yield are opposites

Say you hold a 10% bond. If new bonds pay 12%, nobody wants your stingy 10% one — so to sell it, you must drop the price until its effective return matches the market. Rates up → old bond prices down. Rates down → prices up. The three forces that drive yields: the borrower's creditworthiness, inflation, and short-term interest rates.

⊞ The Reckoner · Bond Price ↔ Yield See-Saw
🔑 Lock It In

Price ↑ ⇒ Yield ↓ (and vice-versa) — a see-saw. Fixed coupon, moving price: the only way the yield can change is through the price.

💡 Did You Know?

The world bond market is worth around $130 trillion — bigger than global stock markets — and it's grown chiefly on government deficits. The US Treasury bond is considered the safest, most liquid security on earth.

In 2020, the total value of negative-yielding bonds (where investors pay issuers to borrow) peaked at over $18 trillion — roughly 20% of all investment-grade debt. Japan's 10-year government bond has spent most of the past decade yielding below zero, meaning buyers are guaranteed to lose money if held to maturity.

🏛 Real Story

Why "gilts"? The original certificates issued by the British government literally had gilded (gold) edges — a mark of their gold-standard safety. The nickname stuck for centuries.

Week 07 · Semester 2

Forex Markets I

The big idea An exchange rate is just the price of one currency in another — set by supply and demand, and tugged over time toward purchasing power parity.

Without a foreign-exchange market, cross-border trade would need a single universal money and there'd be no easy way to invest abroad. Forex is where currency prices are set.

Three market types

  • Spot — immediate price, immediate delivery (our focus).
  • Futures — standardised contracts, fixed dates and amounts.
  • Forwards — custom contracts tailored as needed.

Purchasing Power Parity (PPP)

The same basket of goods should cost the same everywhere once converted. If a basket is $357 in the US and £229 in the UK, the exchange rate "should" be about $1.56 = £1. If it isn't, arbitrage nudges it back. PPP is a long-run anchor — it ignores transport costs, trade barriers and the fact that inflation is only one of many forces.

⊞ The Reckoner · Purchasing Power Parity

Arbitrage in action

If £1 = €2.00 in London but £1 = €1.90 in Singapore, buy euros cheaply in London and sell them dearer in Singapore: €2,000 costs £1,000 in London and fetches ~£1,052 in Singapore — a 5.26% riskless profit. The buying and selling pushes both rates together until the gap closes.

⊞ The Reckoner · Arbitrage Profit

Where rates move freely they're floating; central banks sometimes intervene to fix a rate by buying or selling their own currency.

🌍 In Plain Life

PPP is the "Big Mac" idea: if a burger costs $5 in New York and £4 in London, the implied rate is $1.25 = £1. When the real rate drifts far from that, something — eventually — has to give.

🔑 Lock It In

Spot = now, Futures = standardised, Forwards = bespoke. Arbitrage is the market's self-correction: buy cheap here, sell dear there, until "here" and "there" agree.

💡 Did You Know?

The "carry trade" — borrowing in a low-interest-rate currency (like Japanese yen) and investing in a high-rate one (like Australian dollars) — has been a staple of forex markets for decades. When it unwinds, as it did dramatically in 2007, the resulting volatility can shake global markets.

China holds the world's largest foreign-exchange reserves at over $3.2 trillion — enough to cover roughly 18 months of imports. Switzerland's central bank, meanwhile, has accumulated over $1 trillion in foreign assets (mostly US stocks and bonds) to prevent the Swiss franc from appreciating too strongly against the euro.

Week 08 · Semester 2

Forex Markets II — Volatility & Hedging

The big idea Floating currencies are volatile, volatility hurts real businesses, and most of it comes from speculation — so the hunt is on for ways to tame it.

Picture exporting a Vietnamese drink to the US: your costs are in VND, your sales contracts in USD, locked years ahead. A swing in the USD:VND rate could wipe out your profit — or scare you off the venture entirely.

Three exposures to forex risk

  • Transaction risk — a signed foreign-currency deal loses value between agreement and settlement.
  • Translation risk — foreign assets shrink in home-currency terms (an accounting effect).
  • Economic risk — the whole operating environment shifts as rates move.

Taming it

History tried fixed systems: Bretton Woods (1944–72) pegged currencies to the dollar and the dollar to gold; the European ERM kept currencies in a narrow band. Firms hedge with forwards (lock in a future rate) and options — a call is the right (not obligation) to buy at a strike price, a put the right to sell, for a fee called the premium. Interest-rate parity links spot and forward rates to the interest-rate gap between two countries.

James Tobin's proposed Tobin tax — a tiny 0.1–0.2% levy on spot forex — would deter rapid speculators (a daily trader would face ~50% a year) without hurting genuine trade. Critics doubt it could be set high enough to stop speculation without also harming real business.

⊞ The Reckoner · Tobin Tax Bite
🏛 Real Story

In 1992 the ERM broke down — famously when George Soros bet over $10 billion against the pound and "broke the Bank of England", forcing the UK out of the mechanism in a single day (Black Wednesday). Fixed systems fail when markets decide the peg is wrong.

💡 Did You Know?

By 2002 daily forex turnover hit $1.8 trillion — yet daily world trade was just ~$90 billion. Only about 5% of currency trading relates to trade; the other 95% is speculation, and 80% of it is reversed within days.

Soros's Quantum Fund reportedly made over $1 billion profit on Black Wednesday alone — the single largest one-day gain by a hedge fund at the time. The UK Treasury spent roughly £27 billion trying to defend the pound's ERM peg before conceding defeat.

🔑 Lock It In

Three exposures: Transaction (a deal), Translation (the books), Economic (the whole business). And options: Call up to buy, Put down to sell.

Week 09 · Semester 2

Equity Markets I — Why Bubbles Happen

The big idea Shares are ownership of a company — but their prices swing between cold calculation and pure crowd psychology.

Equities are the most visible securities in finance; by 2018 the world's stocks were worth over $91.8 trillion. Companies issue them to raise capital; people buy them to grow wealth, earn the equity risk premium (the reward over the risk-free rate), or simply to speculate. The Efficient Market Hypothesis says prices reflect all information — yet markets clearly misbehave.

When price leaves reality: bubbles

A speculative bubble is when price departs from intrinsic value, driven by psychology and herding rather than fundamentals. Several thinkers explain it:

  • Keynes's "biggest fool" theory — you don't buy what's worth most; you buy what you think others will pay more for. Intrinsic value becomes irrelevant.
  • Minsky's Financial Instability Hypothesis — euphoria builds, debt and risk-taking creep up, then recognition, panic and revulsion.
  • Greenspan's "irrational exuberance" (1996) — when the Fed chair merely uttered the phrase, global markets dropped within hours.

Trading happens via IPOs (first public issue) on the primary market and then on the secondary market, where market makers profit from the bid-offer spread.

🏛 Real Story

In the 1720 South Sea Bubble, even Sir Isaac Newton lost about £20,000 (over £2 million today). His verdict: "I can calculate the movement of the stars, but not the madness of men." Bubbles go back further still — to Dutch tulipmania in the 1500s.

💡 Did You Know?

The "Magnificent Seven" tech giants — Apple, Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, Microsoft, Nvidia and Tesla — held almost a third of the S&P 500's ~$51.8 trillion value, with valuations near their highest since the dot-com era. Echoes of bubbles past?

During the dot-com bubble (1995–2000), the NASDAQ Composite rose from under 1,000 to over 5,000 — then crashed 78% by 2002. Pets.com, perhaps the era's most infamous failure, burned through $300 million in funding and collapsed within 268 days of its IPO. Yet from those ashes emerged Amazon, eBay, and Google — survivors that now dominate the global economy.

🔑 Lock It In

"Biggest fool" theory: you only need someone more foolish to buy it from you. A bubble is a chair-shortage waiting to happen — fine until the music stops.

Equity Markets II — Exchanges & Indexes

The big idea Meet the great exchanges — and grasp the subtle fact that how you weight an index quietly changes the story it tells.

The big exchanges

  • NYSE — the world's largest, tracing to the 1792 Buttonwood Agreement; equity value over $44 trillion. Survived "Black Monday" 1987 and introduced circuit breakers.
  • NASDAQ — founded 1971, the world's first electronic stock market (no trading floor), heavy with tech: Apple, Amazon, Microsoft, Tesla.
  • LSE — one of the oldest; launched the FTSE 100 in 1984 and the junior AIM market in 1995.

How indexes are weighted (this is the exam favourite)

Same two stocks · opposite conclusions

Cap-weighted (S&P, NASDAQ, FTSE)

The Dow (DJIA) — oldest in use (1896) — is price-weighted: pricey stocks sway it regardless of company size. The S&P 500, NASDAQ and FTSE are market-cap-weighted, so they track the economy more faithfully.

⊞ The Reckoner · Index Weighting

Why the market moves the economy

A crash hits via the wealth effect (poorer households spend less), harder capital-raising for firms, tighter credit (collateral loses value), dented confidence, and global spillovers. That's why the stock market doubles as a predictor of economic activity.

🏛 Real Story

On Black Monday, 19 October 1987, the Dow plunged about 22% in a single day. The shock gave us circuit breakers — automatic trading halts that still pause markets in free-fall today.

🔑 Lock It In

Dow = Price-weighted (Pricey stocks rule). S&P/NASDAQ/FTSE = Cap-weighted (Companies' size rules). Price-weighting is a quirk of history; cap-weighting tracks the real economy.

💡 Did You Know?

High-frequency trading (HFT) firms now execute over 50% of all US equity volume, holding shares for milliseconds rather than years. The fastest trading systems can execute orders in less than one millionth of a second — literally faster than the blink of an eye.

The NYSE's market cap of ~$44 trillion exceeds the entire US GDP. In 2023, the combined market cap of just Apple and Microsoft surpassed the entire London Stock Exchange. The "January effect" — where small-cap stocks historically outperform in January — has been documented since 1942, though it has weakened as markets became more efficient.
