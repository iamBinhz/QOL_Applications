Various Banking Systems Traditional Universal Banking (German Model) In its Historical Form (Pre-2000s): Complete integration of commercial and investment banking, large equity stakes in industrial companies (e.g. Deutsche Bank held major stakes in Daimler-Benz, Siemens…) Over time, universal banking systems changed: Reduced industrial holdings due to capital requirements, More arms-length relationships with industrial companies

---

Various Banking Systems B. Market-Based System (US Model) In its historical form (1933-1999): Clear separation between commercial and investment banks (e.g. Citibank  was strictly commercial vs. Morgan Stanley, only an investment bank) Over-time, market-based system changed too: Large financial conglomerates became a mix of commercial and investment banking (e.g. Morgan Stanley expanded into retail banking)

---

Various Banking Systems Universal Banking Advantages: - One-stop shopping for financial services, Economies of scope, Stable corporate relationships and more reliable affordable credit for industrial growth Universal Banking Disadvantages: Complex risk management and Potential conflicts of interest Market-Based System Advantages: Competitive market for services, Clear separation of risks Market-Based System Disadvantages: Higher transaction costs, Multiple banking relationships were needed (credit not as convenient for industrial companies)

Differences between the UK and the US Banking System 1. Reserve requirements: Reserve requirements are used by the central banks to increase or decrease the amount of money in the economy and influence interest rates. US banks are required to hold a fraction of their funds in reserve with the Fed The current “reserve requirement ratio” in the US is 0% (since March 2020) in response to covid-19 crisis. There is no reserve requirement in the UK system. There are minimum capital requirements in line with international standards.

---

2. Difference in Electronic Payment systems The development of computer systems and the internet has made electronic payments a more cost-effective method over paper cheques or money. The US is still far behind Europe in the use of this technology. Americans write close to 10 billion cheques every year. In Europe, however, two-thirds of noncash transactions are electronic. Europeans have been using giro payments for decades. Giro payment: bank transfers initiated by the payer (similar to debit car payments)

---

3. Differences in Deposit Insurance Schemes Deposit insurance protects bank depositors, in full or in part, from losses caused by a bank&apos;s inability to pay its debts. The U.S. was the first country to introduce a national deposit insurance system. The Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC) was founded in 1933 after the bank failures of the Great Depression. Its goal was to restore confidence in the liquidity of bank deposits and the banking system Currently, deposits up to $250,000 are fully covered. In the UK, the system was established after the 1980s. Currently deposits up to £85,000 are protected.

Safety Net Index around the world Higher values (longer bars) denote more generosity in deposit insurance schemes. Potential “ moral hazard ” issue? Moral hazard arises when people&apos;s actions do not reflect the full cost of their actions. With deposit insurance, people could put their money into banks that made excessively risky loans without fear of losing their money should the bank fail. Source: Demirguc-Kunt, Asli, Edward Kane and Luc Laeven, Deposit Insurance Database , World Bank Policy research working Paper, 2014

Chinese Banking System Immediately after the foundation of People&apos;s Republic in 1949, banks were nationalized and consolidated. During 1980s:  banking system expanded and diversified From 1979 to 1985, the volume of deposits and loans nearly tripled By 1987 the banking system included the People&apos;s Bank of China, Agricultural Bank of China, Bank of China, China Investment Bank, China Industrial and Commercial Bank, People&apos;s Construction Bank, Communications Bank, People&apos;s Insurance Company of China, rural credit cooperatives, and urban credit cooperatives. All banks were state owned. This helped China’s economic growth a lot.

Chinese Banking system 4 largest Chinese Banks are state-owned enterprises. Other banks have some state ownership, as well as private ownership through shares sold in stock markets  (in Shanghai, Shenzhen and Hong Kong stock markets). “ Big Four” have 50% of the market share (relatively concentrated market) Banks dominate the Chinese financial system, providing the loans amounting to about 128% of Gross Domestic Product (GDP) in 2012 (compared to 48% for the US). The banking sector is more focused on state-owned enterprises, large development and infrastructure projects and politically favoured businesses. This focus of banking sector helped  fuel China’s economic development.

Chinese Banking system Downsides to having a state-owned banking system: 1) Borrowing is hard for small businesses. Small businesses end up relying on loan sharks, pawn brokers, informal cooperatives of locals lending to each other…channels outside of formal banking sector (sometimes referred as shadow banking). China&apos;s leaders are well aware that there&apos;s too much infrastructure spending and too little spending by consumers. 2) For large banks, non-performing loans (NPL) was a serious problem in mid-2000s, shaking the confidence in the Chinese banking system. Due to excessive loans to state-owned enterprises before 2000. The Chinese government injected a lot of funds into the banking system to strengthen it.
