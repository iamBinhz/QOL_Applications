Interest in Abrahamic Religion 
Judaism 
Based on the grand religious principle, loan is offered to another person to please God, not for a reciprocal gain. 
Any other motivation including making profit out of offering loan is prohibited. 
However, a review of the history of Judaism shows that interest-based transactions with foreigners have served 
as a source of a major revenue. 
Christianity 
Interest was first banned in 325 (in Canon 17). 
The ban remained in place up to the tenth century and applied to most commercial activities and monetary 
relations (Rubin, 2010). 
The church has imposed different forms of punishment against those who exercised this practice (Meeks, 2011). 
The interest which has been legalized and banned in different times has been eventually justified after going 
through different phases (Kaleem and Lewis, 2013).

1- What is Islamic Finance? 
Islamic Finance refers to the provision of financial services in accordance 
with Islamic law (Shari’ah), principles and rules. 
Shari’ah is the Devine prescriptions in the form of faith and belief, laws 
and moral norms broadly classified into two strands: 
• ibadah (worship and devotional practices) 
• muamalat (civil transactions) 
• The primary sources of the Shari’ah are the Qur’an (Holy book) and 
Sunnah (sayings, practices and tacit approvals of the Prophet 
Muhammed) 
• The central ruling body is the International Islamic Fiqh 
Academy (IIFA) 
Islamic finance – a different paradigm? 
a.Prohibition of Interest (Riba) 
Conventional Finance: Interest is a core element of conventional 
finance, where lenders receive predetermined interest payments on 
loans. 
Islamic Finance: Interest (riba) is strictly prohibited in Islamic 
finance. Instead, profit and loss-sharing mechanisms are used to 
generate returns on investments, promoting risk-sharing. 
What is Riba? 
• Definition of Riba 
• Riba refers to any guaranteed interest or profit on a loan or investment without exposure to risk or 
effort. 
• It’s considered an unjust gain, as it involves earning money through money alone, without 
productive activity. 
• Why is Riba Prohibited? 
• Exploitation : Riba creates an imbalance where one party benefits without effort, while the other 
bears the financial burden. 
• Social Inequality : Interest-based systems often contribute to widening economic disparities, 
benefiting lenders at the expense of borrowers. 
• Discourages Productive Investment : Emphasis on earning interest can lead to reduced 
investment in productive or real economic activities.

Islamic finance – a different paradigm? 
b. Prohibition of Gharar 
Gharar is excessive uncertainty that needs to be eliminated in all 
Islamic finance transactions. 
The reason for such a prohibition is to protect both parties entering 
into a transaction from dealing with uncertainty that may cause 
harm and unfairness to them. 
Example: Selling a product with unclear specifications or uncertain 
outcomes, such as selling "a fish in the sea." 
Islamic finance – a different paradigm? 
c. Prohibition of Maisir (gambling) 
All forms of activities that are considered to be gambling are prohibited in 
all Islamic finance transactions. 
A transaction is considered as gambling if it fulfils three requirements: 
1) All parties involved in the transaction shall contribute financially; 
2) The party who will gain in the transaction will be determined using 
luck; 
3) One party gaining in that transaction will make all the other parties 
lose the financial amount they have contributed. 
Islamic finance – a different paradigm? 
d. Risk and Return Sharing 
Conventional Finance: Conventional finance typically involves fixed 
interest rates, resulting in a limited sharing of risks and returns between 
borrowers and lenders. 
Islamic Finance: Islamic finance encourages risk-sharing through trade 
and partership, where both parties share profits and losses. This aligns 
incentives and promotes fair distribution of wealth and economic growth.

e. Asset-Backed Financing 
• Conventional Finance: Conventional loans are often not directly linked to 
specific assets or economic activities. 
• Islamic Finance: Islamic financing transactions must be backed by 
tangible assets or services, ensuring a direct link to real economic

• Islamic Finance: Islamic finance prioritizes ethical and socially responsible

Murabaha (Cost-Plus Financing) 
• Definition : A sale contract where the bank purchases an asset on behalf 
of the client and sells it to them at a profit margin, which is agreed upon 
in advance. 
• Mechanism : 
-The bank buys the asset requested by the customer. 
-The customer buys the asset from the bank at a cost-plus agreed profit. 
-Payment can be made in installments or a lump sum. 
• Key Feature : Transparency in profit and cost, avoiding interest. 
• Usage : Often used in asset financing, real estate, and vehicle purchases. 
Ijara (Leasing) 
• Definition : A lease agreement where the bank buys an asset and 
leases it to the client for a fixed rental payment over a specified 
period. 
• Mechanism : 
-The bank owns the asset and provides the client the right to use it. 
-The client pays rent, and the ownership may transfer at the end of the 
lease. 
• Key Feature : Rental payment instead of interest, ownership can 
transfer through a separate contract (Ijara wa Iqtina). 
• Usage : Commonly used in real estate and equipment leasing. 
Musharaka (Partnership Financing) 
• Definition : A partnership agreement where both the bank and the client 
contribute capital to a project and share profits and losses based on their 
contributions. 
• Mechanism : 
-Both parties invest funds. 
• -Profits are distributed according to an agreed ratio, while losses are 
shared based on capital contribution. 
• Key Feature : Risk-sharing, no fixed interest; partners are actively involved 
in the project. 
• Usage : Used in business ventures, real estate development, and large 
projects.

Sukuk (Islamic Bonds) 
• Definition : Sukuk are Islamic financial certificates similar to bonds, representing 
partial ownership in a tangible asset, project, or business venture, compliant with 
Shariah law. 
• Mechanism : 
-Asset-Backed: Sukuk holders own a portion of the underlying asset and are 
entitled to returns generated by it, rather than receiving interest. 
-Profit and Loss Sharing: Returns are based on asset performance, with no 
guarantee of a fixed income, reflecting the principles of profit and risk-sharing. 
-Maturity: At the end of the term, the asset is often sold, and proceeds are 
distributed back to investors. 
• Common Issuers : Governments, financial institutions, and corporations for 
funding development projects in a Shariah-compliant way. 
Islamic Social Finance 
Zakat - is a compulsory obligation for all Muslims who are eligible to pay it and the 
money received as zakat shall only be spent or disbursed to those who are the legal 
recipients of it. 
Sadaqat or Infaq – voluntary charity. 
Waqf - the appropriation of the wealth from private ownership with the intention 
of dedicating its use to charitable purposes in perpetuity. 
Islamic Microfinance - mechanism through which the financially excluded 
population is provided with financing opportunities in an affordable manner. Some 
Islamic banks offer microfinance. 
Qard Hassan - a benevolent loan in Islamic finance, where the lender does not 
charge interest or profit on the loan.
