Banking Without Securitization In traditional banking, banks used to “Originate and hold” loans on their balance sheet until maturity Loans are illiquid assets e.g. mortgages are typically for 25 years Deposits are liquid liabilities e.g. even savings accounts are typically for 12 months A “maturity mismatch” arises in bank balance sheet Liabilities are short-term and liquid, assets are long-term and illiquid

---

Banking Without Securitization In traditional banking: When the bank needs financing to meet the mismatch, it could borrow money i.e. issue debt The banks has to pay an interest on that debt The interest paid to investors depends on the overall bank riskiness.

---

Banking with Securitization Banking with Securitization is also called – “Originate-to-distribute model” Securitization refers to converting illiquid loans like mortgages into liquid, marketable securities that can be sold to other institutions. Instead of “holding” loans and their risk on their own books, securitization allows banks to pass them onto others and “distribute” them.

---

Securitization – step by step step Initial lender of the loan: the originator bank Loans are passed on to a special-purpose entity (SPE) or a special-purpose vehicle (SPV) - separate from the originator bank The SPV issues new securities, using the pledged loans to securitize the offering. A credit rating agency rates new securities so that investors have a better idea what the new financial securities are worth An investment bank underwrites these securities New securities are then sold in the money and capital markets with the help of an underwriter

Securitization of securities! Began around 1999 Investors who bought different tranches of MBS, and other ABSs pooled them again and created new securities called CDOs (collateralized debt obligations) CDOs were tranched similarly: ‘senior’ CDOs: backed by highly rated MBS and other ABS, ‘mezzanine’ tranches pooled together a higher proportion of junior tranches. Benefits of Securitization Diversifies a bank’s credit risk exposure Creates liquid assets out of illiquid assets Transforms these assets into new sources of capital Allows the bank to better manage interest rate risk Allows the bank to generate fee income

Housing bubble in the US House prices started increasing fast after 2000. One reason was low interest rates Another reason was lax lending practices of mortgage lenders

Housing bubble in the US A growing number of American banks began selling mortgages to so-called sub-prime borrowers. These were the individuals to whom banks would normally refuse loans – people with very low and insecure incomes who represented a very bad risk. The average credit score was 624 for subprime borrowers. Prime mortgage borrowers 742.

---

Securitizing Sub-Prime Mortgages Why would profit maximising banks willingly lend to people they knew would almost certainly fail to repay their loans? Mortgages are loans secured against the houses they are used to purchase. In the event of a default, banks could recover their money by selling the mortgaged property. Banks could charge sub-prime borrowers higher fixed interest rates – typically 2% more Sub-prime borrowers were also offered very low variable interest rates known as teaser rates- for the first year or two of their loans. Banks used securitization techniques to finance all of this. Securitization became a major profit source for financial intermediaries

---

Crunch Time In the mid 2000s, the US interest rates started to rise, just as many sub-prime borrowers were coming off their teaser rates. Thousands of sub-prime borrowers began defaulting on their mortgages. Many houses were repossessed by the banks and put up for sale, causing house prices to fall sharply to levels below the value of the original mortgage loans. Since the value of the and MBS and CDOs depended on the value of the houses that underpinned them, banks and other financial institutions realised that they were holding almost worthless CDOs. That’s when banks stopped lending to each other in the interbank market, causing a systemic LIQUIDITY problem that is known as the credit crunch.

The role of credit rating agencies (CRA) in the process "Big Three" CRAs—Moody's, Standard &amp; Poor's, and Fitch Ratings Many investors such as pension funds are restricted by law to invest only in the safest securities. i.e. "triple-A". CRAs became “arbiters of risk,” as everyone, even regulators, came to rely on their risk assessment. However, CRAs faced a conflict of interest in their fee structure. They get paid by issuers of securities for whom higher ratings mean higher earnings. They are publicly traded firms with pressures to grow and increase profits. The triple A security pool had more than 3 trillion dollars of sub-prime loans in it by 2007. These were all downgraded to "junk" status after the crisis in 2010.

---

The role of investment banks in the process Investment banks are the underwriters of the securitization process. They purchased, repackaged and sold all types of ABS, MBS, CDOs…etc and earned commission income from the sales. The collapse Lehman Brothers triggered the crisis. Many were bailed out by the governments. Conflict of interest was again the problem e.g. Goldman Sachs was marketing risky CDOs to its clients, whilst also placing large bets against the losses in those CDOs (remember credit default swaps). When the markets collapsed Goldman made millions in profits from the CDSs. They made money by betting against their own products and clients.
