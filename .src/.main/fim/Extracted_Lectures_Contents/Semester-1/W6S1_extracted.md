JP Morgan 
• Founded by J.P. Morgan in 1871 
• 1895: Famously rescued the U.S. Treasury by organizing a syndicate to 
supply the federal government with gold 
• 1907: J.P. Morgan personally helped stop the Banking Panic of 1907, 
cementing the bank's reputation 
• 1935: Forced to separate commercial and investment banking due to 
Glass-Steagall Act, creating Morgan Stanley as an investment bank 
• 2000: Merged with Chase Manhattan Bank to form JPMorgan Chase 
• 2008: Acquired Bear Stearns and Washington Mutual during financial 
crisis 
• Known for its conservative approach to risk management 
Goldman Sachs 
• Founded by Marcus Goldman in 1869 
• 1896: Joined NY Stock Exchange 
• 1906: Managed first IPO (Sears, Roebuck and Co.) 
• 1929: Created Goldman Sachs Trading Corp, which failed in the crash 
• 1999: Went public after 130 years as a partnership 
• 2008: Converted to bank holding company during financial crisis 
• 2023: Attempted strategic pivot to focus more on consumer banking, then 
largely retreated 
• Famous for its executives turning politicians in the US (e.g., Robert Rubin, Henry 
Paulson, Steven Mnuchin, all became Secretary of the Treasury) 
• Known for involvement in controversies and riskier ventures (compared to JP 
Morgan)

Underwriting Stocks and Bonds 
When a company decides to go public with Initial Public Offering (IPO), they create and 
sell shares in the stock market. This is called borrowing through equity. 
Alternatively, a company can borrow by debt by issuing bonds and selling them in the 
corporate bond market. 
Investment banks make sure that the company raises enough funds by: 
• Giving Advice 
• Explaining current market conditions to help determine what type of 
security (equity or debt) to offer 
• Assisting in determining when to issue, how many, at what price 
• Filing Documents 
• Registration with authorities (filing) is required. 
• A portion of the registration statement known as the prospectus is made 
available to the public. 
Underwriting Stocks and Bonds 
Underwriting can take three forms: 
1. Firm commitment 
• The investment banker buys the entire offering at a fixed price and then resells 
the offering to the market. 
• An underwriter may form an underwriting syndicate to diffuse part of the 
underwriting risk. 
• Placement of a tombstone in the financial press. 
2. Best Efforts: the underwriter does not buy the issue, instead makes its “best effort” 
to sell the entire issue. 
3. Private Placements: The entire issue is sold to a small, select group of investors 
• Less regulatory requirements 
• More common in issuing debt (bonds) 
Using Investment Bankers to Distribute Securities to 
the Public in the US stock markets 
SEC is the regulatory authority in the US – Securities and Exchange Commission 
In the UK – This is the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA)

Investment Banks as “Market Makers” 
Secondary market function 
• Market Maker is someone who ensures liquidity in the 
market. 
• Investment Banks “make the market” in a certain security by 
buying and selling it for their own account. 
• An investment bank may promise an issuer that, in return 
for getting the mandate to underwrite and distribute its 
bonds, the bank will be prepared to buy and sell them 
(make a market in them). 
Equity research 
Equity Research and brokerage (sales and 
trading) 
There are also independent equity research firms 
They make money from subscriptions to their research reports 
Investment banks with equity research department make money from 
brokerage trades (i.e. give advice to their clients to buy/sell and execute 
the following transactions on a commission) 
Clients are other financial institutions and companies (i.e. institutional 
investors: Hedge Funds, Pension Funds, Insurance Companies, Mutual 
funds, …etc).

• when a firm sells an entire division (or maybe the entire company), 
enlisting the aid of an investment banker.

potential targets (although not both in the same deal).

Source: Financial Times 
Investment Banking Business 
Model 
Fee Structure & Revenue Streams 
1. Advisory Fees 
• M&A fees: Typically 1-2% of deal value (lower percentage for larger 
deals) 
• Success-based fees: Only paid when deals close 
• Retainer fees: Monthly/annual fees for ongoing advisory services 
2. Underwriting Fees 
• IPO underwriting: Usually 4-7% of total offering 
• Bond issuance: 0.1-3% depending on complexity and risk 
• Follow-on offerings: Lower fees than IPOs (2-4%) 
3. Trading Revenue 
• Market making 
• Proprietary trading (though restricted post-Dodd-Frank) 
• Sales and trading commissions 
Career hierarchy in Investment 
Banking 
1. Analyst (Entry Level): Recent graduates with base salary of £50-65K, and 
bonus ranging from 30-100% of base. London salaries typically higher. 
Key duties: financial modelling, presentations, research 
2. Associate: After 3-4 years of experience with direct promotion or after a 
relevant Masters degree. Base salary: £80-120K. Bonus: 50-100% of 
base. Responsibilities: Team management, client interaction 
3. Senior Associate 
4. Vice President 
5. Director/Executive Director 
6. Managing Director
