What Is a Financial Crisis? An Information Perspective We learned about moral hazard and adverse selection problems in week 2 Asymmetric information creates barriers between savers and firms with productive investment opportunities. A financial crisis occurs when information flows in financial markets experience a particularly large disruption. Financial markets may stop functioning completely.

Stages of Financial Crises in Advanced Economies Factors causing financial crises -----lead to information problems -----which lead to further disruptions

---

Stage One: Initial Phase Financial crises can be caused by: Credit Boom and Bust Financial institutions rapidly expand lending, risk management standards decline. Banks compete aggressively and extend credit to increasingly risky borrowers, supported by inflated collateral values. Eventually, loan losses accumulate, borrowers default and asset values fall, forcing banks to cut back on lending (deleveraging).

---

Stage One: Initial Phase Why Do Credit Booms happen in the first place? elimination of restrictions, lack of regulation introduction of new types of loans or other financial products i.e. “innovation” low interest rates –encourages excessive lending and borrowing

---

Stage One: Initial Phase What’s does this have to do information problems? Banks don&apos;t just lend money - they&apos;re experts at figuring out which businesses are trustworthy borrowers and monitoring how they use loans. During a credit bust, when banks stop lending, this crucial evaluation system breaks down. Banks can&apos;t tell good borrowers from bad ones anymore (Adverse Selection) No one&apos;s watching to make sure borrowers use money responsibly (Moral Hazard) Even healthy businesses can&apos;t get loans because there&apos;s no one left to properly evaluate them. This makes the economic downturn worse as good businesses can&apos;t get the funding they need to operate and grow.

---

Stage One: Initial Phase Financial crises can also be caused by: Asset- price boom and bust : During booms, asset prices (like stocks or real estate) rise above their real value, creating a bubble. When this bubble pops, it triggers a dangerous chain reaction: Falling asset prices reduce the value of businesses and their collateral, making them more likely to take risks since they have less to lose (the moral hazard problem) Financial institutions suffer losses on these same assets, forcing them to reduce lending and sell more assets. This selling pushes prices down further, creating a negative spiral Bursting of asset bubbles hits both borrowers and lenders at the same time Companies become riskier borrowers just as banks become less able to absorb losses, leading to a credit crunch and potential financial crisis.

---

Stage One: Initial Phase Increase in uncertainty triggers the crisis Credit booms and asset-price booms create fragility through high debt and inflated asset prices. When uncertainty hits, it triggers a vicious cycle: No one knows who&apos;s safe to lend to Asset prices fall as confidence drops Falling prices create more uncertainty Banks stop lending This causes more asset sales and price drops Booms set up the dominoes, and uncertainty (often from a big firm&apos;s failure) knocks them down. Each falling domino (asset prices, lending, confidence) knocks down the next, deepening the crisis.

---

Stage Two: Banking Crisis When banks&apos; financial health declines (bad loans, falling asset values) Depositors get scared, people rush to withdraw money from all banks (&quot;bank run&quot;) Banks must sell assets quickly at fire-sale prices to get cash These rushed sales make banks&apos; finances even worse The Damage: Banks stop lending as they fight for survival Good borrowers can&apos;t get loans (adverse selection) Desperate firms take bigger risks (moral hazard) Economy suffers as credit freezes - recovery takes years as trust must be rebuilt When people suspect some banks are sick but don&apos;t know which ones, they run from all banks - creating a self-fulfilling crisis that hurts even healthy banks.

---

Stage Three: Debt Deflation When prices fall sharply but debt amounts stay fixed: Asset values drop but loan amounts remain the same Real debt burden increases for borrowers E.g. House worth £200K → £150K, but £180K mortgage remains This Creates a Dangerous Cycle: Borrowers struggle with higher real debt loads More likely to default or take risks (moral hazard) Banks reduce lending further Economic activity remains depressed for a long time

Case: The Great Depression Recession turned into something far worse, when severe droughts in 1930 led to a sharp decline in agricultural production. Reduced agricultural incomes, declining land values, loan defaults by farmers put banks in financial difficulty Caused loss of confidence among depositors Caused bank runs Between 1930 and 1933, one-third of U.S. banks went out of business For more than two years, the Fed sat idly, doing nothing to help banks or the economy

Case: The Great Depression Adverse selection and moral hazard in credit markets became severe. Firms with productive uses of funds were unable to get financing. As seen in the next slide, credit spreads increased from 2% to nearly 8% during the height of the Depression in 1932. A credit spread is the extra interest that borrowers must pay compared to a government, showing how nervous lenders are about getting their money back.
