Different Forms on Money 
1. Cash (banknotes and coins) 
• Created under the authority of Bank of England 
• Around 3% of the circulating money supply 
2. Electronic money 
• Bank of England creates electronic money for the banking system 
• Banks have their own accounts with Bank of England 
• Money in these accounts are called central bank reserves 
• Banks create electronic money for their customers

Bank of England’s Definitions of 
Money Supply 
Narrow money (also known as M0, high-powered money, monetary base, 
or central bank money) 
1. Notes and coins in circulation (i.e. Cash ) 
2. Reserve balances at the Bank of England 
Broad Money 
M1: Cash plus current accounts 
M2: M1 plus savings accounts 
M3: M2 plus repurchase agreements, money market funds, 
and debt securities up to two years (not commonly used anymore) 
M4: M3 plus other deposits at UK banks and building societies.

Liabilities 
Current accounts are the lowest cost funds for the bank – 
• safe and liquid, but offer low interest to customers. 
Savings accounts are the primary source of funds 
• Highest cost of funding, but most stable for bank 
Borrowings : funds from the central bank, other banks, and corporations 
• More volatile than other liabilities 
Bank Capital : is the source of funds supplied by the bank owners. 
Assets 
Reserves are funds held in account with the central banks 
Cash items are

Loans : a bank ’s income-earning assets, not very liquid

Money Multiplier Model 
● Suppose Hank deposits the loan in his regular account with Bank B 
● The deposits at Bank B rise by $9 million and its reserves also rise by $9 
million 
● Bank lending has expanded the money supply by $9 million . 
Now, Bank B must hold some required reserves ($900,000) but can lend out 
the other amount ($8.1 million) in a loan to Jack’s Chevy Dealership. 
Bank B Balance Sheet 
Bank B Balance Sheet 
Money Multiplier Model 
● If Jack’s Chevy Dealership deposits the loan in its account at 
Bank C, the money supply just increased by an additional $8.1 
million 
● Making loans that are then deposited into a deposit account 
increases the money in circulation 
○ Banks are required to hold only a fraction of their deposits 
○ loans end up deposited back into banking system 
○ which increases deposits and the money supply.
