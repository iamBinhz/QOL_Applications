Risk Management 
• The ability to properly and effectively identify, assess, 
monitor, and manage each type of risk is critical to a 
bank’s financial soundness and profitability. 
• Diversification of business activity helps reduce the impact 
that volatility in any particular area may have on profits. 
• Risk management policies and procedures are evolutionary 
and are subject to ongoing review and modification.

Measuring Credit Risk 
These ratios assess a bank’s exposure to loan losses and how well it manages credit 
risk. 
 Loan Loss Reserves / Gross Customer Loans & Advances (%) 
o Measures how much of the loan book is reserved for potential loan losses. 
o Higher value = Lower risk (indicates better provisioning for bad loans). 
 Loans & Advances to Customers / Total Assets (%) 
o Measures the proportion of assets tied to loans, indicating loan concentration 
risk. 
o Higher value = Higher risk (more exposure to credit risk).

Managing credit risk 
• The credit risk of an individual loan concerns the losses the 
bank will experience if the borrower does not repay the 
loan. 
• The credit risk of a bank’s loan portfolio concerns the 
aggregate credit risk of all the loans in the bank’s portfolio. 
• Banks must manage both dimensions effectively to be 
successful. 
Managing credit risk 
• Adverse selection in loan market: those likely to default are 
more likely to take loan (borrowers with risky project gain) 
• Moral hazard in loan market: borrowers may have incentive to 
engage undesirable activities (likely to default) 
• Bank manage credit risk by: 
1) Screening and monitoring 
● Screening: screen bad credit risk from good one by collecting 
reliable information . 
● Specialization lending: lend to local firm or specific industry 
● Monitoring and enforcement of restrictive covenants : monitor 
borrowers’ activities and limit the borrower from engaging in 
risky activities. 
Managing credit risk 
2) Long-term customer relationship 
If borrowers have long-term engagement with banks, this 
reduce information collection costs and make it easier to 
screen out bad credit risks. 
3) Loan commitments – provide loan with special term for 
existing clients. (e.g: a guarantee that the lender will continue 
to provide funds up to the credit limit at a certain interest rate) 
4) Collateral – provide security to banks in the event of default 
(e.g. property, car) 
5) Credit rationing – provide borrowers with smaller loan to 
reduce default risk

Solvency Risk 
• Bank with high capital will still have a positive capital value. 
• But bank with low capital is not solvent (bankrupt). 
• This example shows how capital becomes a cushion against the decline 
in the value of bank assets. 
• So, to be resilient against failure, banks need a large enough buffer of 
capital. The riskier a bank’s assets, the more capital it needs. 
• However, there is trade-off between safety and returns to equity 
holders: 
-Benefits the owners of a bank by making their investment safe 
-Costly to owners of a bank because the higher the bank capital, the 
lower the return on equity

Solvency Risk 
• Process of Insolvency 
1. Loans go bad (cannot be repaid). 
2. Losses erode the capital base. 
3. Liabilities surpass assets. 
4. Bank becomes insolvent. 
• Impact 
• Loss of stakeholder confidence. 
• Potential collapse of the bank. 
• Broader financial system risks. 
Measuring Solvency Risk 
These ratios assess how well-capitalized a bank is to absorb financial shocks. 
 Total Equity / Total Assets (%) 
o Indicates how much of the bank’s assets are funded by shareholders rather 
than debt. 
o Higher value = Lower risk (stronger equity position). 
 Total Capital Adequacy Ratio (%) 
o Measures the bank’s capital buffer against risk-weighted assets. 
o Higher value = Lower risk (stronger capital position).

Liquidity Risk 
• Liquidity problems arise due to interactions between 
funding and the asset side of the balance sheet — when a 
bank does not hold sufficient cash (or assets that can easily 
be converted into cash) to repay depositors and other 
creditors. 
• The demands for liquidity include accommodating deposit 
withdrawals and paying other liabilities as they come due.

Measuring Liquidity Risk 
These ratios measure how well a bank can meet its short-term obligations. 
 Loans & Advances to Customers / Deposits & Short-Term Funding (%) 
o Measures reliance on deposits for lending. 
o Higher value = Higher risk (suggests potential liquidity issues if too many 
loans are issued relative to deposits). 
 Liquid Assets / Deposits & Short-Term Funding (%) 
o Measures the proportion of deposits backed by liquid assets. 
o Higher value = Lower risk (stronger liquidity position).

Interest Rate Risk 
• It is the riskiness of earnings and returns that is associated with 
changes in interest rates. 
• If a bank has liabilities that are more sensitive to interest rate changes 
than assets: 
- an increase in interest rates will reduce bank profits. 
- a decrease in interest rates will raise bank profits. 
• Basic gap analysis: 
= (rate sensitive assets – rate sensitive liabilities) × Δ interest rates 
= Δ in bank profit

Example for Bank A 
- Has £20 million assets that are rate-sensitive (with interest rates that change at least once a year), and £80 million 
assets are that have fixed-rate (with interest rates that remain unchanged for over a year). 
- Has £50 million of rate-sensitive liabilities and £40 million of fixed-rate liabilities. 
- Suppose that interest rates rise by 5% on average, from 10% to 15%. 
- The income on the assets rises by £1 million (5% x £20 million of rate-sensitive assets), while the payments on the 
liabilities rise by £2.5 million (5% x £50 million of rate-sensitive liabilities). 
- The Bank’s profits now reduce by £1.5 million (£1 million - £2.5 million). 
- However, if interest rates fall by 5%, we will find that the Bank’s profits increase by £1.5 million.

Managing Operational Risk 
• The financial crisis precipitated a wave of regulatory fines and enforcement actions on misselling, 
questionable mortgage-foreclosure practices, financial crimes, London Inter-bank Offered Rate (LIBOR) 
fixing, and foreign-exchange misconduct. 
• Compared to other risks, operational risks is more complex as it involves diverse risk types that involves 
different groups of people (compliance, financial crime, cyber risk, and IT risk). 
• Operational risk can be mitigated through corporate governance. 
• Operational-risk management requires oversight and transparency of almost all organizational processes 
and business activities. 
• This means that as long as people, systems, and processes remain imperfect, operational risk cannot be 
fully eliminated. 
• The Basel Committee on Banking Supervision (BCBS) has highlighted the importance of operational risk. 
• They can be mitigated by good corporate governance –putting in place foundational elements such as 
loss-event reporting and risk-control self-assessments and holding capital buffer against operational risk.

Dilemma: Profitability versus Safety 
• A bank must balance the demands of three constituencies: 
shareholders —mainly interested in profitability 
depositors —mainly interested in safety 
regulators —mainly interested in safety 
• One way for a bank to increase expected profits is to take on 
more risk. However, this can jeopardize bank safety.

Solvency and Liquidity 
• Solvency: Maintaining the momentum of a going concern, 
attracting customers and financing. 
• A firm is insolvent when the value of its liabilities exceeds 
the value of its assets. 
• Liquidity: The ability to fund deposit withdrawals, loan 
requests, and other promised disbursements when due. 
• A bank can be profitable and still fail because of illiquidity. 
35 
Conflicting Demands 
• A bank must balance profitability, liquidity, and solvency. 
• Bank failure can result from excessive losses on loans or 
securities -- from over-aggressive profit seeking. But a bank 
that only invests in high-quality assets may not be profitable. 
• Failure can also occur if a bank cannot meet liquidity 
demands. If assets are profitable but illiquid, the bank also has 
a problem. 
• Bank insolvency often leads to bank illiquidity.
