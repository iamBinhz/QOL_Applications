Economic Role of Money Market (MM) 
• The money market is a market for liquidity. 
• Liquidity is stored in MM by buying MM securities. 
• Liquidity is bought in MM by selling securities (borrowing). 
• Provides a place for Central bank’s reserve transactions

Commercial paper 
• Unsecured short-term instrument of debt, issued primarily by 
corporations 
• Promises to the holder a sum of money to be paid in a set 
number of days – it is a promissory note 
• Buyers include other corporations, insurance companies, 
pension funds, governments and banks 
• Average maturity of about 40 days; the normal range is 30–90 
days, but can be as long as 270 days 
• Normally issued at a discount . 
Commercial paper (cont) 
• Discount rate (and yield) higher than for Treasury bills 
• Available only to the most respected corporations 
• Some companies employ in-house teams to do the selling, other 
issuers ask dealers 
• Can be done in multiple currencies 
• Extendable commercial paper (rolling over debt). 
Commercial Paper Issuance in the UK

A feature of the 2008 Credit Crunch was the unwillingness

issuance, as companies turned to short-term sources of 
finance.

Repurchase agreements (Repos) 
• A repo is a way of borrowing for a few days using a sale 
and repurchase agreement in which securities are sold 
for cash at an agreed price with a promise to buy back the 
securities at a specified (higher) price at a future date 
• Used very regularly by banks and other financial 
institutions to borrow money from each other 
• This market is also manipulated by central banks to 
manage their monetary policy 
• The term for repos is usually between 1 and 14 days

Certificates of deposit (CDs) 
• Issued by banks when funds are deposited with 
them by other banks, corporations, individuals or 
investment companies 
• Maturities can be any length of time between a week 
and a year (typically 1–4 months) 
• Lots ranging from £50,000 to £500,000 in the UK, 
or $100,000 to $1 million in the US 
• Non-negotiable CDs must be held by the depositor 
until the CD reaches maturity 
• Negotiable CD traded in a secondary market. 
Money Market Instruments: The Interbank Market 
• Financial institutions borrow/lend short-term funds , usually for a period of 
one day. 
• Used by banks to meet short-term needs 
• Banks main alternative to interbank borrowing is to borrow from the Bank of 
England, the lender of last resort. 
• The interbank market is also used by other financial institutions like 
insurance companies and pension funds that wish to earn a return on their 
surpluses of ready cash. 
• Since the 1980s, the role of the interbank market has broadened considerably. 
• The interest rate established in the interbank market – the London Interbank 
Offered Rate ( LIBOR ) – has been used as the benchmark rate for setting 
interest rates on a wide variety of loan products – credit card loans, car loans, 
some mortgages – in Britain and around the world. 
The US Interbank Market is called Federal Funds Market
