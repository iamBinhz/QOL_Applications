Hedge Funds 
• A special type of investment fund 
• Different from typical mutual funds, as follows: 
• High minimum investment, averaging around $1 million 
• Long-term commitment of funds is required 
• The fund invest in various sectors, such as land, stocks, derivatives, currencies, and commodities. 
• It often uses borrowed money to create leverage and multiply returns. 
• High fees: typically, 1-2% of annual asset management fee and 20% of performance fee on profit. 
• Lower legal and regulatory constraints 
• Flexible mandates permitting use of shorting and derivatives 
• Aggressive investment styles that allow concentrated positions in securities offering exposure to 
credit, volatility, and liquidity risk premiums. 
• Relatively liberal use of leverage.

Leverage 
• Let’s say you have £ 100 cash with you. But at the same time, you are 
able to borrow £900 more (assuming interest rate at 8%). So now the 
total money that you have is £1000. 
• Assume you invest £1000 and you earn 10% return on it in one year (So it 
now goes up to £1,100). 
• After you repay your debt of £900 and the interest of £72 (8% of £900), 
you are left over with £128 (£1,100-£900- £72). 
• What is your return? 
• [(£128 - £100) / £100] * 100 = 28% return on your initial capital of £100.

Hedge Fund Strategies 
Equity long-short - equity securities and equity-like securities such as swaps 
and options (with the objective of outperforming the traditional equity 
benchmark) 
Equity Market Neutral funds - aim to create positive returns regardless of 
the overall market trend and it is achieved by hedging against market 
factors and by creating a zero net-exposure. 
Short selling – strategy that bet and profit from a decline in security’s price 
(borrowing a security and sells it to the open market with the plan to buy it 
later at a lower price) 
Arbitrage Funds - exploits the differential between the cash and derivative 
market to generate returns (prices in derivative market reflect an 
anticipated value of the stock at a set time in the future). 
Hedge Fund Strategies 
• Convertible arbitrage - is an equity long-short investing strategy that 
typically involves the simultaneous purchase of convertible securities and 
the sale of the underlying common stock (aims to capitalise on pricing 
inefficiencies between a convertible security and the stock). 
• Fixed income arbitrage – aim to profit from pricing anomalies between 
fixed income securities regardless of the market condition. 
• Global macro strategy – aims to exploit broad market swings in the global 
market, and it is based upon the interpretation of macroeconomic and 
geopolitical events (focus on systematic risk of markets). 
• Event driven – e.g. managers consciously purchase debts of a troubled 
company in hope to profit if the company turns around.

Hedge Funds 
• Investors should conduct extensive due diligence before 
they commit money to any hedge fund. 
• Understanding which strategies the fund uses, as well as its 
risk profile, is an essential first step. 
• Also, need to understand whether or not the hedge fund 
aligns with the investor's overall investment. 
Private Equity Investments 
• An alternative to investing via public securities is private 
equity (PE) investments. 
• PE owns companies privately (instead of buying shares of a 
company in the stock exchange, these investments are done 
behind the closed doors, through private transactions). 
• Private equity (PE) refers to investments made into private 
companies or the buyout of public companies, resulting in 
their delisting from public stock exchanges. 
Private Equity Investments 
Key Characteristics: 
• Ownership: Investors acquire significant ownership stakes in 
companies. 
• Capital Source: Typically comes from institutional investors and 
high-net-worth individuals. 
• Goal: Enhance company value through strategic management, 
operational improvements, and growth initiatives. 
• Exit Strategies: Includes Initial Public Offerings (IPO), mergers, 
or private sales. 
• Most common types of PE are: 
• venture capital funds 
• capital buyouts

PE type 1: Venture Capital Firms 
• These firms provide funds for start-up companies 
• Often become very involved with firm management and provide expertise 
• Description of Industry 
• Typically limited partnerships 
• Examples of venture-backed firms include Apple, Microsoft, Starbucks, Facebook, WhatsApp, 
Snapchat… 
• Have resulted in job creation, economic growth, and international competitiveness. 
• First U.S. venture capital firm was established in 1946. 
• Funding has shifted over time from wealthy individuals to pension funds/corporations (this is one of the few 
risky investments pension funds are permitted). 
• Corporate funding of venture capital projects increased when many companies reduced their investment in 
their own in-house R&D in favour of outside start-up companies (successful projects were acquired).

Venture Capitalists Reduce 
Asymmetric Information 
• Managers of start-ups may have objectives that differ significantly from profit 
maximization. 
• Venture capitalists can reduce this information problem in several ways 
1) Long-term motivation - as opposed to bank loans or bond financing, venture capital 
firms hold an equity interest in the firm. The firms are usually privately held (illiquid) - 
venture capital investment horizons are long-term (no return expected for a few years). 
2) Sit on the board of directors ( not passive investors) - actively attempt to add value to 
the firm through advice, assistance, and business contacts. 
3) Disburse funds in stages, based on required results - i f development stalls or markets 
change, funds can be withheld. 
4) Invest in several firms, diversifying some risk - the risk of loss is significantly lowered. 
Life of Venture Capital Deal 
1. Fundraising 
• Usually have a portfolio target amount 
• Venture firm solicits commitments, usually less than 100 per deal 
• Minimum commitment is usually high 
2. Investment phase 
• Seed investing - invest in a firm before it has a real product or is even clearly organized as a company 
(60%) 
• Early-stage investing – invest in a firm that is little further along in its life cycle (25%) 
• Later stage investing - provide funds to help the company grow to a critical mass to attract public 
financing (15%) 
3. Exit 
• The goal of a venture capital investment is to help nurture a firm until it can be funded with 
alternative capital. 
• Usually IPO or through mergers and acquisitions

PE Type 2: Leveraged buyouts (LBO) 
• A public company is taken private. 
• Why go private? 
• Avoid regulations 
• Provides flexibility and ability to avoid public scrutiny of earnings. 
Also helps attract top talent no longer interested in the life of a 
public-company CEO. 
• Tax advantages, and high compensation for partners. 
Private Equity Buyouts 
• Lifecycle of a Private Equity Buyout 
• Investors pledge money (usually $1 million or more) and intent to 
leave money in partnership for 5+ years. 
• Partners identify an opportunity, buy it, and then manage its future 
(typically hire a CEO for day-to-day operations). 
• The company is then sold to the public via an IPO.

Private Equity Deals in 2024 
• In 2024, private equity (PE) firms significantly increased their activity in Europe, capitalizing on the region's economic 
downturn to acquire large companies at reduced valuations. 
• The total value of European buyout deals exceeding $1 billion reached approximately $133 billion , marking a 78% 
increase from 2023. 
• This growth rate surpassed the global increase of 29% for similar deals, which totaled $242 billion . 
• Notable transactions during the year : 
$6.9 billion consortium agreement for investment platform Hargreaves Lansdown 
$5.5 billion deal by Thoma Bravo to take the British cybersecurity company Darktrace private 
Several factors contributed to this surge in PE activity: 
Economic Conditions: Europe faced a challenging economic outlook characterized by modest growth forecasts, political 
instability, and geopolitical threats. These conditions, combined with the strength of the U.S. dollar, made European assets 
more attractive to U.S. private equity funds. 
Targeted Economies: Stable European economies, such as the U.K., the Nordics, and Germany, became focal points for 
private capital providers seeking reliable investment opportunities amidst broader economic uncertainties. 
This trend underscores the strategic moves by private equity firms to leverage favorable market conditions and acquire 
valuable assets at advantageous valuations.
