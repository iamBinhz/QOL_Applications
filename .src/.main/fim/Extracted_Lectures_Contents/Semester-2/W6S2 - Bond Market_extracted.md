## Page 12

Bond Yield vs. Price 
As bond prices increase, bond yields fall . Why? 
Suppose an investor purchases a bond that matures in five years with a 10% annual coupon rate and a face 
value of £1,000. 
• If interest rates rise above 10% 
• The original bond still only makes a coupon payment of £100, which would be unattractive to 
investors who can buy bonds that pay more than £100. 
• If the original bond owner wants to sell the bond, the price must be lowered so that the coupon 
payments and maturity value will bring a higher return. 
• If interest rates were to fall , the bond's price would rise because its coupon payment is more attractive. 
• For example, if interest rates fell to 7.5% for similar investments, the bond seller could sell the 
bond for £1,101.15. The further rates fall, the higher the bond's price will rise, and the same is true 
in reverse when interest rates rise.

## Page 14

Yield to maturity calculation 
Current yield is different from redemption yield (Yield to maturity) because it only considers 
a single year return 
• Yield to maturity takes account of any capital gain/loss at redemption. 
e.g. a bond with 10 years to redemption with a coupon of £6 is currently trading at £120 (i.e. 
price) 
The current yield is 6/120 = 0.05 = 5%. 
The holder receives the face value of £100 at redemption, leaving her with a capital loss of 
£20. In percentage terms, capital loss = £20/£120 = 0.167= 16.7%. 
If 10 years to maturity, the annualized percentage capital loss = 0.167/10 = 0.0167 
Yield to maturity = 0.05 – 0.0167 = 0.033 = 3.3% 
• Alternatively, simply sum the coupon payments as £6 x 10 = £60, subtract the capital loss 
of £20 = £40, divide by time to redemption = 40/10 = 4 and divide by 120 =0.33 or 3.3%.

## Page 16

Yields and maturities 
• Bonds are classified into types according to their initial maturities. 
• Shorts – having up to 5 years to redemption. 
• Mediums – having up to 15 years to redemption. 
• Longs – having more than 15 years to redemption. 
• Ultra longs – longs with at least 40 years to redemption. 
• Because bonds pay a nominal coupon, the real value of which is reduced by inflation, 
investors will demand higher yields from bonds with a long time to maturity, reflecting the 
greater uncertainty about future rates of inflation.

## Page 17

2 - Bond valuation 
• The main 3 factors that drive bond yields are: the creditworthiness of the 
borrower, inflation rate and short - term interest rates. 
• The bond market instils discipline in governments as creditworthiness 
affects borrowing costs for them. 
• Short - term borrowing tends to be cheaper but could be riskier for 
borrowers than long - term borrowing, as it relies on the sentiment of 
lenders. 
• Given that repayments to bondholders are fixed, inflation can corrode the 
purchasing power of these fixed payments, increasing yields. 
• Yields on US government bonds can affect other bonds.
