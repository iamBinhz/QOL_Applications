### Slide 6

Types of risk A low standard deviation means that all the possible returns are right around the mean. Low risk . A high standard deviation means that there is a lot of variability in the possible return. High risk . Total Risk Firm-Specific Risk (Unsystematic) Related to a specific company, sector, or industry. Arises from internal or external factors associated to the company’s operations and management. Examples: Liquidity risk; Workers strikes; Credit risk, CEO retirement. Controllable and avoidable via DIVERSIFICATION (averaging out). Market Risk (Systematic) Uncontrollable and unavoidable. Is uncertainty inherent to the entire market or economy. Arises from macroeconomic factors. Impacts almost all companies collectively. Examples: Inflation risk; Interest rate risk.

### Slide 7

Risk and Diversification Any rational investor would look for: The highest possible expected returns The lowest possible risks Investors tend to create a portfolio of different shares to eliminate all controllable risk, i.e. unsystematic risk. Diversification refers to creating a portfolio of a variety of investments to reduce unnecessary risks. 7

### Slide 8

Measuring Investment Risk and Return Measuring Risk: Standard Deviation Measures the dispersion of returns around the expected return. A higher standard deviation indicates higher volatility and risk. Beta Coefficient Measures a stock’s volatility relative to the overall market. A beta greater than 1 indicates higher volatility than the market, while a beta less than 1 indicates lower volatility. Measuring Return: Historical Return The actual return earned on an investment over past periods. Expected Return The anticipated return on an investment, based on probabilistic outcomes and future projections.

### Slide 24

Capital Market Efficiency Capital market history suggests that the market values of equities and bonds can fluctuate widely from year to year. Why does this occur? New information arrives, and investors reassess asset values based on that information. Efficient capital market: a market in which security prices reflect available information.

---

### Slide 25

Efficient Markets Hypothesis (EMH) The idea that financial markets quickly and accurately reflect all available information in share prices. This means prices are generally fair- not too high, not too low. Why does this happen? Lots of investors and analysts compete to find mispriced shares. When new information appears, investors act on it, and prices adjust very quickly. Key implication: In an efficient market, it is very hard to “beat the market” consistently, because prices already reflect the information everyone knows. Source: Financial times, 2025

---

### Slide 26

Forms of Market Efficiency Weak-form efficiency Prices reflect all past price information. Looking for patterns in historical prices (technical analysis) won’t help you beat the market. Semi-strong-form efficiency Prices reflect all public information (news, earnings, reports). Analysts using public data cannot consistently find underpriced or overpriced shares. Strong-form efficiency Prices reflect all information, including private/inside information. Almost certainly not true in real life — insider information still matters.
