### Slide 6

Hedging and Financial Risk Management What is Hedging? Hedging (Immunisation): Reducing exposure to price and rate fluctuations Purpose: Protect cash flows, profits, and firm value Achieved using financial risk management techniques Financial Engineering Direct hedging is not always possible Managers design solutions using existing instruments Known as financial engineering Derivative Securities Financial instruments whose value depends on another asset Examples: Options (shares, currencies) Futures and forwards Commodity contracts

### Slide 7

Managing Financial Risk Manager’s Role: Identify price changes that affect firm value most Assess both: Direct effects (costs, revenues) Indirect effects (market demand, customer behaviour) What Is a Risk Profile? A risk profile shows: Relationship between price/rate changes And changes in firm value Example: Wheat Market Wheat producer: Higher wheat price → higher firm value Food processor (buyer): Higher wheat price → lower firm value Hedging Through Contracts Firms can agree on: Quantity, Price and Delivery date This flattens the risk profile and reduces exposure Perfect hedging is rare — goal is risk reduction , not elimination.

### Slide 8

Short-Term vs Long-Term Financial Risk Short-Term Risk (Transactions Exposure) Temporary price shocks Examples: Oil price spikes Exchange rate swings Interest rate changes Why Hedge Short-Term Risk? Protects cash flows Prevents financial distress Improves operational stability 8 Financial risk management helps firms: Shield against short-term volatility Gain time to adjust to long-term market changes Long-Term Risk (Economic Exposure) Permanent structural changes Examples: New technology Industry decline Long-term price shifts Cannot be fully hedged Firms must adapt business strategy instead

### Slide 9

Hedging with Forward Contracts What Is a Forward Contract? A forward contract is a legally binding agreement to: Buy or sell an asset At a fixed price agreed today With delivery on a future settlement date Contract Obligations Buyer (Long position) Must take delivery and pay agreed price Benefits if market price rises Seller (Short position) Must deliver asset and receive agreed price Benefits if market price falls Key Characteristics: Customised (over-the-counter) contracts, No upfront payment; Zero-sum game : one party’s gain = other party’s loss Pay-off Profile (Concept) Shows gains and losses caused by price changes Buyer gains when prices increase Seller gains when prices decrease 9

### Slide 11

Hedging with Forward Contracts Why Firms Hedge Using Forwards: To reduce exposure to unexpected price movements Commonly used for: Commodities (oil, metals) Foreign exchange Interest rate exposure Example: Electricity Utility Using Oil Problem: Oil price rises → higher production costs and Electricity prices are regulated → limited ability to pass on costs Solution: Utility buys an oil forward contract Result: If oil prices rise → gains on forward offset higher costs If oil prices fall → lower costs offset losses on forward Overall risk exposure is reduced Important Limitations Hedging removes: Downside risk Upside potential gains Credit risk exists (counterparty may default at settlement)

### Slide 12

Hedging with Futures Contracts What Is a Futures Contract? A futures contract is similar to a forward contract, but with one key difference: Gains and losses are settled daily , not only at maturity. Marking to Market (Daily Settlement) Contracts are revalued every trading day; Profits are credited and losses are debited daily; Reduces default (credit) risk compared to forwards Why Futures Are Widely Used Traded on organised exchanges Standardised contracts High liquidity and transparency Lower counterparty risk Types of Futures Contracts Commodity futures: Oil, gold, wheat, cocoa, copper Financial futures: Shares, bonds, stock indices, currencies Real-World Example Cryptocurrency futures (e.g. Bitcoin, Ethereum): Often used for speculation rather than hedging real economic exposure

### Slide 14

Hedging with Futures &amp; Practical Issues How Firms Hedge Using Futures Hedging logic is identical to forward contracts Firm takes a futures position with an opposite risk exposure Daily gains and losses are settled via broker margin accounts Cross-Hedging When an exact futures contract does not exist: Firms hedge using a closely related asset Example: Hedging jet fuel prices using crude oil futures➡ Called cross-hedging Closing Futures Positions Most futures contracts are not held to maturity Firms usually: Enter contract ➡ Later take the opposite position ➡ Cancel exposure before delivery

### Slide 15

Hedging with Futures &amp; Practical Issues Maturity Risk (Contract Roll-Over Risk) Long-term exposures hedged with short-term futures may create risk Example: Metallgesellschaft case — mismatch in hedge maturity caused large losses (nearly went bankrupt in 1993 after losing more than $1 billion in the oil markets) Key Takeaways Futures reduce credit risk via daily settlement Cross-hedging allows flexibility Poor hedge design can increase risk instead of reducing it

### Slide 16

Forwards vs Futures Contracts Feature Forward Contract Futures Contract Definition Agreement to buy/sell an asset at a future date for a price agreed today Similar to forward, but gains/losses are settled daily Settlement Only at contract maturity Daily settlement (marking to market) Customization Fully customisable (quantity, delivery date, asset type) Standardised (exchange-specified terms) Trading Venue OTC (over-the-counter) Organised exchange Credit Risk Higher (counterparty may default) Lower (daily marking reduces default risk) Liquidity Typically lower High liquidity, easy to enter/exit Hedging Use Effective for specific, customised exposures Effective for general, standardised exposures; allows cross-hedging Delivery Often leads to actual delivery Rarely physical delivery; positions closed before maturity Profit/Loss Realization Only at maturity Realized daily

### Slide 17

Hedging with Swap Contracts Definition: A swap contract is an agreement between two parties to exchange specified cash flows at set intervals . Essentially, a swap is a series of forward contracts . Main Types of Swaps: Currency Swap: Exchange cash flows in different currencies. Example: US firm borrows in USD, German firm borrows in EUR → swap payments to hedge exchange rate risk. Interest Rate Swap: Exchange fixed for floating interest payments. Example: Firm A wants fixed, Firm B wants floating → swap payments via a dealer. Commodity Swap: Exchange payments based on commodity prices. Example: Airline hedges fuel costs using jet fuel swaps. Key Points: Swaps are not traded on exchanges ; they rely on swap dealers . Swap dealers intermediate trades , balance risk, and maintain a swap book .

---

### Slide 18

Interest Rate Swap Example Company A can borrow at a floating rate equal to EURIBOR plus 1 per cent, or at a fixed rate of 10 per cent. Company B can borrow at a floating rate of EURIBOR plus 2 per cent, or at a fixed rate of 9.5 per cent. Company A desires a fixed-rate loan, whereas Company B desires a floating-rate loan. Clearly, a swap is in order. Swaps hedge risk and improve financing terms . Dealer role is crucial for liquidity and risk management.

---

### Slide 19

Hedging with Option Contracts Definition: An option contract gives the owner the right, but not the obligation to buy or sell an asset at a specified price for a set period. Key difference from forwards/futures: obligation vs right . Types of Options: Call Option – right to buy at a strike price. Put Option – right to sell at a strike price. Exercise Styles: American Option: exercise any time until expiration. European Option: exercise only at expiration. Option Premium: Buyer pays the seller for the right. Unlike forwards, options have an upfront cost. Pay-off Profiles: Call buyer profits if asset price rises above strike price. Put buyer profits if asset price falls below strike price. Sellers face the opposite outcome — zero-sum game.

---

### Slide 20

Hedging with Option Contracts Hedging Example: Firm wants to protect against adverse price movements . Strategy: buy a put option → eliminates downside risk, retains upside potential. Acts like an insurance policy , but with a cost (premium). Applications: Commodity Price Risk: futures options on wheat, oil, etc. Exchange Rate Risk: put options to protect against adverse FX movements. Interest Rate Risk: Cap: call on interest rates → protects floating-rate loans from rising rates. Floor: put on interest rates → ensures minimum rates. Collar: combine cap and floor → maintain rate within a band. Swaptions: option to enter a swap → convert floating to fixed-rate loans. Key Takeaways: Options allow risk reduction while keeping potential gains. Flexible hedging tool across commodities, currencies, and interest rates .

### Slide 21

Financial Management in Practise Use of Derivatives: Derivatives are often not visible in financial statements , making their use hard to observe. Surveys show large firms use derivatives more than small firms. Most popular derivatives: Foreign currency derivatives Interest rate derivatives Global Usage Patterns: Usage varies widely across firms and industries. Hedging intensity = number of instruments used by an average firm. Why Use Derivatives? Reduce cash flow variability . Lower financial distress costs . Manage exposure to interest rate, currency, and commodity risks . Speculation vs Hedging: While derivatives are mainly for hedging, some firms use them to speculate on future prices . Typically used where financial distress costs are high and capital market access is limited . Key Takeaway: Derivatives are a powerful risk management tool , especially for large firms facing volatile markets.

### Slide 23

Key Takeaways Identify and manage risks that can affect cash flows and firm value. Forwards: Lock in future prices; zero-sum; reduce risk, eliminate upside. Futures: Like forwards, but marked-to-market daily; reduce default risk. Swaps: Exchange cash flows; used for currency, interest rate, and commodity risk. Options: Right, not obligation, to buy/sell; protect downside, retain upside. Derivatives are widely used by large firms to manage complex exposures and can reduce financial distress costs by stabilizing cash flows. May also be used for speculation, not just hedging. Effective risk management balances risk reduction and opportunity, using the appropriate financial instruments.
