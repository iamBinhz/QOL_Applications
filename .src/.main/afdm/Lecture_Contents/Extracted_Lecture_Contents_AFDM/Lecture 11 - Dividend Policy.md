### Slide 5

Dividend “A payment made out of a firm’s earnings to its owners, in the form of either cash or stock ” Basic Types of Cash Dividends: Regular dividends – recurring payments as part of normal profits. Extra dividends – additional payments above the regular dividend. Special dividends – one-off payments from exceptional profits. Liquidating dividends – return of capital when a company is partially or fully winding up. 5

### Slide 6

Chronology of a Cash Dividend Payment Declaration Date – Board approves the dividend (e.g., £1 per share). Ex-Dividend Date – Usually 2 business days before the record date. Buy stock before this date → entitled to dividend (“cum dividend”). Buy stock on/after this date → previous owner gets dividend (“ex-dividend”). Record Date – Corporation lists shareholders eligible for the dividend. Payment Date – Dividend is actually paid to shareholders. 6

### Slide 7

Other Dividends types Stock Dividends Stock dividends are a non-cash form of dividends. With a stock dividend (also known as a bonus issue of shares ), the company distributes additional shares of its common stock to shareholders instead of cash. Example: a company announced a 5% stock dividend 7 Before If a shareholder owns 100 shares with a purchase price of $10 per share, the total cost basis would be = 100 shares x $10 = $1,000. After a 5% stock dividend, The shareholder would own 105 shares of stock at a total cost of $1,000. = $1000/105 shares = $9.5238 per share

### Slide 8

Other Dividends types SHARE REPURCHASES The purchase, by a corporation, of its own shares of equity; also known as a buyback. Methods of Repurchase: Open Market Purchases – company buys shares like any other investor. Tender Offer – company offers to buy back a fixed number of shares at a set price (often above market price). Targeted Repurchase – company buys shares directly from specific shareholders. 8

### Slide 9

Example For Share Repurchase BP PLC facts: Equity value = £1,000,000 (100,000 shares, £10 each) Extra cash available = £300,000 Earnings = £49,000 → EPS = £0.49 9 Option 1: Pay Dividend Pays £3 per share dividend (£300,000 ÷ 100,000) Share price falls to £7 (ex-dividend) Shareholder with 100 shares: Value = 100 × £7 = £700 Cash dividend = 100 × £3 = £300 Total wealth = £1,000 (same as before). Option 2: Repurchase Shares Buys back 30,000 shares at £10 each Shares left = 70,000 New EPS = £49,000 ÷ 70,000 = £0.70 Price per share stays £10 Shareholder with 100 shares still has £1,000 value In a “perfect market” (no taxes, no costs), dividends and repurchases give the same wealth outcome.

### Slide 10

Example For Share Repurchase Real-World Considerations: Tax Advantage: Dividends taxed immediately; repurchases taxed only if shares sold (often lower capital gains tax). Signalling &amp; Perceptions: Buybacks may signal confidence in undervalued shares. Trends: Buybacks surged in EU (now &gt; half of dividend value). Declined during crises (e.g., Covid-19), but used by firms like Berkshire Hathaway when share prices were cheap. (Source: Hillier, 2021, pp: 465) 10

### Slide 16

Theories on Dividend Payments Modigliani &amp; Miller (M&amp;M) Dividend Irrelevance: This theory states that dividend patterns have no effect on share values. Company value depends on its investment decisions , not how earnings are split between dividends and retained profits. Cutting dividends today doesn’t destroy value if retained earnings are reinvested well. 16

### Slide 17

Practical Considerations in Dividend Policy Investor-related factors: Signalling – Dividend changes send messages (growth vs. trouble). Trust issues – Shareholders may doubt directors’ optimism. “Bird in the Hand” preference – Many prefer cash now over uncertain future income. Clientele effect – Different investors prefer different dividend patterns (e.g., pension funds want stable income). 17

### Slide 19

Practical Considerations in Dividend Policy Company-related constraints: Liquidity – Firms may cut dividends to preserve cash. Borrowing covenants – Lenders may restrict dividend payments. Legal rules – No distributable reserves = no dividends. Scrip dividends – Shareholders may choose shares instead of cash (Sometimes tax-efficient or avoid transaction costs). 19 Dividend policy is shaped by both investor expectations and company constraints

### Slide 21

Key Takeaways Dividends are a way of distributing profits to shareholders (cash, stock, or in some cases through repurchases). Dividend policies vary: constant dividend, constant growth, constant pay-out ratio, residual dividend, or no dividend. Dividend models (e.g., Dividend Discount Model, Gordon Growth Model) help value shares based on expected dividend streams. Ex-dividend date determines who receives the dividend (buy before = entitled; buy after = seller keeps it). Dividend vs Repurchase : both return value to shareholders, but differ in predictability, signalling, and tax treatment. Investor perception matters – changes in dividend policy often send signals about management’s view of the company’s future.
