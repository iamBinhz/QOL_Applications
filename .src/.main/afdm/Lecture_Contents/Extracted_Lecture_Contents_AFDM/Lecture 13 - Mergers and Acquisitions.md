### Slide 2

Recap – Financial Risk Management 2 Identify and manage risks that can affect cash flows and firm value. Forwards: Lock in future prices; zero-sum; reduce risk, eliminate upside. Futures: Like forwards but marked-to-market daily; reduce default risk. Swaps: Exchange cash flows; used for currency, interest rate, and commodity risk. Options: Right, not obligation, to buy/sell; protect downside, retain upside. Derivatives are widely used by large firms to manage complex exposures and can reduce financial distress costs by stabilizing cash flows. May also be used for speculation, not just hedging. Effective risk management balances risk reduction and opportunity, using the appropriate financial instruments.

Mergers and Acquisitions Merger – a combination of two or more businesses under one ownership the complete absorption of one company by another, wherein the acquiring firm retains its identity and the acquired firm ceases to exist as a separate entity. Consolidation - a merger in which an entirely new firm is created, and both the acquired and acquiring firms cease to exist. Acquisition or Takeover - one firm acquires the stock of another (the target) or acquire another firm by buying most or all of its assets . (Hillier, 2021) 4

### Slide 8

Acquisition Classification Financial analysts usually classify acquisitions into three main types: 1. Horizontal Acquisition Occurs when a company acquires another company in the same industry. Both firms usually compete in the same market and offer similar products or services. Main aims: increase market share, reduce competition, achieve economies of scale. 2. Vertical Acquisition Involves companies operating at different stages of the production or supply chain. Helps firms improve efficiency, control costs, and secure supply or distribution. 3. Conglomerate Acquisition Occurs when the acquiring and target firms operate in unrelated industries. Often aimed at diversification and spreading business risk. 8

Takeovers Proxy contest an attempt to gain control of a firm by soliciting a sufficient number of shareholder votes to replace existing management. Going-private transactions transactions in which all publicly owned equity in a firm is replaced with complete equity ownership by a private group. Leveraged buyouts (LBOs) going-private transactions in which a large percentage of the money used to buy the equity is borrowed. Often incumbent management is involved. Alternatives to mergers Strategic alliance agreement between firms to co-operate in pursuit of a joint goal. Joint venture typically an agreement between firms to create a separate, co-owned entity established to pursue a joint goal.

### Slide 20

Key Summary M&amp;A involves combining firms through mergers, consolidations, or acquisitions to create value (in theory). Acquisitions can be friendly or hostile, with shareholders playing a crucial approval role. The main types of acquisitions are horizontal, vertical, and conglomerate, each with different strategic motives. Firms pursue M&amp;A for synergies, growth, diversification, economies of scale, and tax advantages but not all deals succeed. The price paid and method of financing (cash, shares, debt) are critical in determining whether value is created or destroyed. Target firms may use defensive tactics to resist unwanted takeovers. Real-world M&amp;A activity shows that strategy, valuation discipline, and execution matter synergy is not guaranteed.
