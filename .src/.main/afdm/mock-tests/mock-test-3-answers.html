Project SolarMax

W1: Annual Depreciation
= (Initial Investment - Scrap Value) / Life
= (£3,600,000 - £400,000) / 5 = £640,000 per year

W2: Total Cash Flows
= 650,000 + 1,100,000 + 1,250,000 + 850,000 + 750,000 = £4,600,000

= Total Cash Flows - Total Depreciation
= £4,600,000 - (5 × £640,000) = £1,400,000

W4: Average Annual Profit
= £1,400,000 / 5 = £280,000

W5: Average Investment
= (Initial Investment + Scrap Value) / 2
= (£3,600,000 + £400,000) / 2 = £2,000,000

Question 1(d) - Conflicting Recommendations Discussion 8 marks

Why investment appraisal methods may conflict:

  1. Time value of money: Payback and ARR ignore TVM; NPV incorporates discounting. Projects with different cash flow timings will rank differently.
  2. Cash flows vs accounting profits: NPV/Payback use actual cash flows; ARR uses accounting profits (including non-cash depreciation). Different measurement bases produce different rankings.
  3. Project scale and size: Smaller investments may have faster payback but lower absolute returns. NPV measures total value added; payback measures speed of recovery.
  4. Treatment of post-payback cash flows: Payback ignores all cash flows after the payback point. A project with large later cash flows may have poor payback but excellent NPV.

How managers should resolve conflicts:

  • Prioritize NPV - it directly measures shareholder wealth creation and incorporates TVM
  • Use Payback as a secondary liquidity/risk screen (useful for cash-constrained firms)
  • Use ARR cautiously - more relevant for performance measurement than capital decisions
  • Consider qualitative factors (strategic fit, flexibility, risk profile)
Marking: 2 marks per valid point explaining why methods conflict (max 4 points = 8 marks). Accept other reasonable explanations with proper justification.

Question 2(c) - Bond Price Relationships 4 marks

Relationship between Coupon Rate, YTM, and Bond Price:

  • Coupon Rate > YTM: Bond trades at a premium (price > face value)
  • Coupon Rate < YTM: Bond trades at a discount (price < face value)
  • Coupon Rate = YTM: Bond trades at par (price = face value)

This bond: Coupon (6%) < YTM (7.5%), so it trades at a discount (£912.14 < £1,000).

Explanation: Investors require 7.5% return but the bond only pays 6% coupons. To achieve the required yield, investors pay less than face value, effectively earning additional return through capital appreciation at maturity.

Question 3(c) - Systematic vs Unsystematic Risk 4 marks

Systematic Risk (Market Risk):

  • Affects the entire market or large segments
  • Cannot be eliminated through diversification
  • Examples: Interest rate changes, inflation, recessions, political events
  • Measured by beta (β)

Unsystematic Risk (Specific Risk):

  • Specific to individual companies or industries
  • Can be reduced/eliminated through diversification
  • Examples: Management changes, product failures, labor disputes

Why only systematic risk is rewarded:

In efficient markets, rational investors hold diversified portfolios. Since unsystematic risk can be "diversified away" at essentially no cost, the market does not compensate investors for bearing it. Only non-diversifiable systematic risk commands a risk premium because investors cannot avoid it regardless of portfolio composition.

Question 3(d) - Limitations of Beta 3 marks

One valid limitation (choose any):

  1. Historical basis: Beta is calculated from past data, assuming historical relationships will persist. Companies evolve (new products, M&A, strategy shifts), making historical beta unreliable for future predictions.
  2. Measurement sensitivity: Beta varies significantly depending on the measurement period, frequency of data (daily/weekly/monthly), and choice of market index.
  3. Single-factor model: Beta only captures market risk; ignores other systematic factors (size, value, momentum) that may affect returns.
  4. Assumes linear relationship: Beta assumes a linear relationship between stock and market returns, which may not hold during extreme market conditions.
  5. Instability over time: Company betas can change substantially as business risk profiles evolve, making point-in-time estimates unreliable.

Step 1: Calculate Market Values
Market Value of Equity (E) = Shares × Price = 2,000,000 × £12 = £24,000,000
Market Value of Debt (D) = £10,000,000 (given)

Weight of Equity (We) = E/V = 24,000,000 / 34,000,000 = 0.7059 (70.59%)
Weight of Debt (Wd) = D/V = 10,000,000 / 34,000,000 = 0.2941 (29.41%)

Step 3: Cost of Equity (Dividend Growth Model)
D0 = £0.80 (most recent dividend)
g = 5% (growth rate)
D1 = D0 × (1 + g) = £0.80 × 1.05 = £0.84
Ke = (D1 / P0) + g = (0.84 / 12) + 0.05 = 0.07 + 0.05 = 12%

Step 4: After-tax Cost of Debt
Pre-tax cost of debt = Current yield = 6%
Tax rate = 25%
Kd(1-T) = 6% × (1 - 0.25) = 6% × 0.75 = 4.5%

Step 5: WACC
WACC = (We × Ke) + (Wd × Kd(1-T))

Question 4(b) - M&M Dividend Irrelevance Theory 5 marks

Modigliani and Miller (1961) Dividend Irrelevance:

The theory states that in perfect capital markets, dividend policy is irrelevant to firm value. The value of a company depends solely on its investment decisions and earning power (the left-hand side of the balance sheet), not on how profits are distributed between dividends and retained earnings.

Shareholders are indifferent between dividends and capital gains because they can create "homemade dividends" by selling shares if they need cash, or reinvesting dividends to buy more shares if they prefer growth.

Key Assumptions (any TWO):

  • No taxes (or equal tax treatment of dividends and capital gains)
  • No transaction costs for buying/selling shares
  • No flotation costs for issuing new shares
  • Perfect capital markets
  • Symmetric information (no signalling effects)
  • Rational investors
  • No agency costs
Marking: 3 marks for explaining the theory, 1 mark per correct assumption (max 2 marks for assumptions).

Question 4(c) - Why Dividend Policy Matters 4 marks

Reason 1 - Signalling Theory:

Dividends convey information about management's expectations of future performance. Managers have superior inside information (information asymmetry). A dividend increase signals confidence in sustainable earnings; a cut signals potential financial difficulties. Since dividends require actual cash outflows, they are a costly and therefore credible signal. Share prices typically react positively to dividend increases and negatively to cuts.

Reason 2 - Tax Clientele Effect:

Different investor groups have different tax situations. Tax-exempt investors (pension funds) may prefer high dividends; taxable individuals may prefer capital gains (often taxed at lower rates or deferred). Companies attract investors whose preferences align with their dividend policy. Changing dividend policy may cause investor churn and selling pressure.

Alternative valid reasons:

  • Bird-in-hand theory: Investors prefer certain dividends over uncertain future capital gains (risk aversion)
  • Agency costs: Dividends reduce free cash flow available for managers to waste on unprofitable investments
  • Transaction costs: Small investors face costs creating homemade dividends, so they prefer actual dividend payments
