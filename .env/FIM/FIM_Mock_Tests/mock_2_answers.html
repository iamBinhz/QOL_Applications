I

Central Banking & Independence

Part A

Main functions of the Central Bank (Bank of England example)

The Bank of England carries out several core functions as the United Kingdom's central monetary authority. Its primary role is to run monetary policy, with the Monetary Policy Committee setting the Bank Rate to meet the inflation target, while the Financial Policy Committee preserves financial stability by stress-testing banks and supporting the lender-of-last-resort facility. Alongside these, the Bank supervises banks and insurers, holds the sole right to issue banknotes in England and Wales, acts as banker to commercial banks and the government, and manages the UK's foreign-currency reserves. Taken together, these functions make the Bank of England the backbone of monetary control, financial stability and payment infrastructure in the UK economy.

Part B

Critical assessment of Central Bank Independence

Central bank independence is widely argued to produce better macroeconomic outcomes than direct political control of monetary policy. Evidence shows that independent central banks deliver lower and more stable inflation without harming growth, because they are shielded from the political cycle in which governments push rates down before elections and cannot be forced to finance government debt by printing money. Critics, however, argue that independence is undemocratic, since unelected experts control a key economic tool, and some studies suggest that low-inflation outcomes may reflect a country's culture rather than institutional design. On balance, independence remains valuable provided it is paired with clear accountability and transparent reporting, especially in today's environment of high inflation and elevated public debt.

II

Exchange Rates & Interest Rates

Part A

How exchange rates are decided under a floating system

Under a floating exchange-rate system, the rate is the price of one currency in terms of another, determined by supply and demand. Market participants include exporters, importers, banks, dealers, governments, speculators, tourists, and central banks smoothing short-term volatility, whose combined trades continuously reprice each currency pair. In the long run, Purchasing Power Parity (PPP) anchors the rate to the relative cost of goods between countries, while arbitrage keeps quotes consistent across financial centres by eliminating persistent price gaps. Consequently, although speculation dominates day-to-day movements, underlying goods prices and arbitrage ultimately shape the equilibrium exchange rate.

Part B

Relationship between interest rates and exchange rates

Interest rates and exchange rates are tightly linked through Interest Rate Parity (IRP). IRP states that the gap between spot and forward exchange rates must equal the interest-rate differential between two countries, so that investing domestically or abroad produces the same return once the forward rate is locked in. In the short run, a rate rise attracts foreign capital and pushes the domestic currency up, while the Fisher Effect ties this to inflation, since high-inflation countries tend to carry high nominal rates but depreciating currencies over time. Therefore, interest-rate policy is one of the most powerful drivers of currency values, operating through both capital flows and inflation expectations.

III

Interest Rates, Bonds & Equities

Part A

How interest rates are set in financial markets

Interest rates in financial markets are set by the interaction of supply and demand for loanable funds. The Loanable Funds Model determines the general rate where the supply from savings, government surpluses and central-bank action meets the demand from consumer credit, business investment and government deficits, while the Fisher Effect clarifies that it is the real rate, stripped of inflation, that signals whether to borrow or save. Rates on individual loans then diverge according to creditworthiness, maturity, liquidity and whether the loan is secured, producing the yield curve, which normally slopes upward but can invert when markets expect lower future rates. In this way, the market blends a single baseline rate with risk-adjusted premia to generate the full spectrum of borrowing costs observed across the economy.

Part B

Impact of interest-rate changes on bond and share markets

Interest-rate changes exert opposing but equally powerful effects on bond and share markets. Bond prices move inversely with rates because coupons are fixed, so rising rates push prices down and falling rates lift them up, while shares fall when rates rise because future cash flows are discounted more heavily, higher borrowing costs compress corporate profits, and investors rotate out of equities into safer bonds. Prolonged periods of low rates can also inflate asset bubbles, explained by Keynes's "biggest fool" theory and Minsky's boom-and-bust cycle, which burst once rates eventually rise. The resulting crash triggers a wealth effect that suppresses consumption, credit and tax revenues, completing the transmission from monetary policy to the real economy.
