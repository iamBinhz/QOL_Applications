## Question 1

### a) Main functions of the Central Bank (Bank of England example)

The Bank of England's main job is to **run monetary policy**, with the Monetary Policy Committee meeting regularly to set the Bank Rate to hit the inflation target. It also **keeps the financial system stable** through the Financial Policy Committee, which stress-tests banks against shocks, and it acts as **lender of last resort**. Other jobs include **supervising banks and insurers**, being the **only body allowed to print banknotes** in England and Wales, acting as **banker to commercial banks and the government**, and **managing the UK's foreign-currency reserves** — all of which raise the question of independence covered in Q1(b).

### b) Critical assessment of Central Bank Independence

**Being independent stops the political cycle**, because governments have reasons to push rates down before elections, causing higher inflation afterwards. Evidence shows independent central banks deliver lower inflation without hurting growth, and they cannot be forced to print money to pay government debts. **Critics say it is undemocratic**, since unelected experts control a key tool, and point out that some low-inflation outcomes may come from a country's culture rather than independence itself. Today, high inflation and high government debt create **tension between fighting inflation and keeping the system stable**, so independence is still useful when paired with clear reporting — and it links to how rate choices set currencies in Q2.

## Question 2

### a) How exchange rates are decided under a floating system

In a floating system, exchange rates are **the price of one currency in another, set by supply and demand**. Buyers and sellers include exporters, banks, dealers, governments, speculators, tourists and central banks smoothing swings. In the long run, **Purchasing Power Parity (PPP)** sets the rate based on the cost of goods across countries, while **arbitrage** keeps rates equal across cities by removing price gaps. Speculation dominates daily trading, which leads into interest-rate parity in Q2(b).

### b) Relationship between interest rates and exchange rates

**Interest Rate Parity (IRP)** links the gap between spot and forward exchange rates to the difference in interest rates between two countries. This means investing at home or abroad should give roughly the same return once the forward rate is locked in. In the short run, a rate rise pulls in foreign money and **pushes the currency up**, while the **Fisher Effect** connects this to inflation, since high-inflation countries tend to have high nominal rates but weaker future currencies. So interest rates drive both currencies and — as Q3 shows — bond and share prices.

## Question 3

### a) How interest rates are set in financial markets

The **Loanable Funds Model** sets the general rate where the supply of loans (savings, government surpluses, central-bank action) meets the demand for loans (consumer credit, business investment, government deficits). The **Fisher Effect** explains that the real rate is the true signal of whether to borrow or save, once inflation is stripped out. **Rates for specific loans** vary with creditworthiness, length, liquidity, and whether the loan is secured — producing the **yield curve**, which normally slopes up but can flip down when markets expect lower future rates, and is the channel through which rates hit bond and share markets in Q3(b).

### b) Impact of interest-rate changes on bond and share markets

**Bond prices move the opposite way to rates**, because coupons are fixed, so when rates rise prices fall and when rates fall prices rise. **Shares fall when rates rise** for three reasons: future cash flows are worth less today, higher borrowing costs cut profits, and investors move money from shares to safer bonds. **Low rates over long periods can cause bubbles** — explained by Keynes's "biggest fool" theory and Minsky's boom-and-bust cycle — and when rates finally rise, the crash triggers a **wealth effect** that cuts spending, credit, and tax income, completing the chain from central-bank policy to the wider economy.
