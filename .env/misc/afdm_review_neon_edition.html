Foundations

Money has a price, and so does time. Learn to move cash across dates and read a set of accounts.

Lecture 02 · Foundations

Time Value of Money I

The big idea A pound today is worth more than a pound tomorrow — so before you can compare cash from different dates, you must move it all to a common point in time.

Your bank offers you £100 cashback: take it today, or in a year. Almost everyone takes it today. Why? Three reasons: you'd rather consume now, inflation erodes future pounds, and the future is uncertain. To tempt you to wait, the bank must offer more — say £115. That extra £15 is the return that makes you indifferent between now and later.

Cash flows at different dates are like cash flows in different currencies: you can't add them until you convert to a common unit. The "exchange rate" between time periods is the interest rate.

Simple vs compound interest

Simple interest pays only on the original principal. Compound interest pays interest on previous interest too — so it snowballs. Over 5 years at 5%, £1,000 grows to £1,250 simply, but £1,276 with compounding. The longer the horizon, the wider that gap yawns.

FV = PV × (1 + r)n
FV future value · PV present value (principal) · r interest rate per period · n number of periods.

Compounding vs discounting

Pushing a present value forward in time is compounding (multiply by (1+r)n). Pulling a future value back to today is discounting (divide by it). Discounting is the engine of almost everything that follows — investment appraisal, bond pricing and share valuation are all just present values.

PV = FV ÷ (1 + r)n
The discount rate r shrinks distant cash; the further away, the smaller it becomes today.

Continuous compounding

If interest is added not yearly or quarterly but infinitely often, the formula limits to FV = PV × ern, where e ≈ 2.718. It's the theoretical ceiling of compounding frequency.

🌍 In Plain Life

Every savings account, mortgage and student loan is this one idea running in the background. When a lender quotes you a rate, they're telling you their personal "exchange rate" between your money today and your money later.

💡 Did You Know?

The Rule of 72: divide 72 by the interest rate to get the years it takes money to double. At 6% that's 12 years; at 9%, just 8. A back-of-the-napkin shortcut for compound growth that's accurate to within a fraction of a year.

💡 Did You Know?

Albert Einstein allegedly called compound interest the "eighth wonder of the world." Whether he said it or not, the math is undeniable: £1,000 at 7% doubles every 10.3 years. Over a 40-year career, that's 4 doublings — turning £1,000 into £16,000.

🔑 Lock It In

Compound = push forward (×). Discount = pull back (÷). Future value multiplies by (1+r)n; present value divides by it. Same lever, opposite directions.

Lecture 03 · Foundations

Time Value of Money II

The big idea Real decisions involve streams of cash, not single sums — and annuity and perpetuity formulas let you value an entire stream in one line instead of discounting every year by hand.

Discounting one cash flow is easy. But a course costs £1,000 next year, £1,500 the year after and £2,000 the year after that — and a pension pays out for decades. You could discount each payment separately and add them up. For long streams that's painful, so we use shortcuts.

Annuities — a constant stream

An annuity is the same cash flow each year for a fixed number of years (a loan repayment, a fixed pension). Its present value collapses to a tidy formula:

PV = C × [ 1 − (1 + r)−n ] ÷ r
C the constant annual cash flow · r the discount rate · n the number of payments.

Growing annuities & perpetuities

If the cash flow grows each year by rate g, it's a growing annuity (think rent that rises 3% a year). If a constant payment lasts forever, it's a perpetuity — and the maths gets even simpler: PV = C ÷ r. A growing perpetuity is PV = C ÷ (r − g), which reappears as the Gordon model for valuing shares.

The frequency trap: nominal vs effective rates

Cash doesn't only arrive annually, and interest isn't only compounded annually. A 5% rate compounded quarterly actually earns about 5.09% a year — the Effective Annual Rate (EAR). When payments are monthly or quarterly, convert the rate and the period count to match the cash flows, or you'll get the wrong answer.

EAR = (1 + r/m)m − 1
m the number of compounding periods per year. More frequent compounding ⇒ a higher effective rate.
⊞ The Reckoner · Annuity vs Perpetuity
🌍 In Plain Life

Choosing between two flats — one £10,000 a year rising 5%, the other £12,000 a year rising 1% — is a growing-annuity problem. The cheaper-looking flat today can cost far more once you discount a decade of rising rent to its present value.

💡 Did You Know?

A credit card "2% per month" is not 24% a year — it's an EAR of about 26.8%, because each month's interest compounds on the last. Frequency quietly makes borrowing dearer than the headline rate suggests.

💡 Did You Know?

The UK government's Consol bonds (first issued in 1751) are perpetual — they never mature. The government just keeps paying the coupon forever. Valuing them is literally the perpetuity formula in action.

🔑 Lock It In

Perpetuity = C ÷ r. Growing perpetuity = C ÷ (r − g). An annuity is just a perpetuity with the tail (everything beyond year n) chopped off and discounted away.

Lecture 04 · Foundations

Financial Statement Analysis

The big idea Raw figures hide the story; a ratio expresses one number against another, stripping out size so you can compare a corner shop with a supermarket — or this year with last.

Company A makes £250m operating profit; Company B makes £125m. Who did better? You can't say — until you learn A turned over £1,500m and B only £500m. As a margin that's 17% vs 25%: each £1 of B's sales kept 25p, against A's 17p. Ratios remove the size problem and let you judge performance, then compare across firms (cross-sectional) or over time (time series).

The five families of ratio

  • Profitability — gross/operating/net margin and Return on Capital Employed (ROCE): how well resources turn into earnings.
  • Investment — EPS, dividend per share, the P/E ratio: how shareholders' stake is performing.
  • Efficiency — inventory days, receivable days, payable days: how hard the assets are working.
  • Gearing (leverage) — debt vs equity and interest cover: the prime measure of financial risk.
  • Liquidity — current ratio and the acid test: can it pay the bills due now?

Liquidity, up close

The current ratio = current assets ÷ current liabilities. The stricter acid test strips out inventory (harder to turn into cash). Interest cover = EBIT ÷ interest; a common rule of thumb says it should clear 1.5×, or lenders get nervous.

⊞ The Reckoner · Liquidity & Margin
🏛 Real Story

In 2020 Morrisons and Sainsbury's looked alike on the shelf, but their ratios told different stories: Morrisons' interest was covered 22.7×, Sainsbury's only 1.65×. Same sector, very different financial risk — exactly what ratios are built to expose.

💡 Did You Know?

Warren Buffett's favourite metric is Return on Equity (ROE) — and he looks for 15% or better over 10 years. It's not just the number; it's the consistency that matters. A firm that swings between 5% and 25% is riskier than one that steady-states at 14%.

🔑 Lock It In

A ratio alone means nothing — it needs a benchmark. Always compare to a peer, the industry, or last year. And remember ratios are backward-looking and can be flattered by accounting choices.

🌍 In Plain Life

The current ratio is your own bank balance versus this month's bills. Below 1.0 means you can't cover what's due without selling something — fine for a day, alarming as a habit.

Lecture 05 · Investment & Risk

Investment Decision Criteria

The big idea A good project returns more than it costs once you respect the timing of cash — which is exactly why NPV outranks payback and ARR.

Capital budgeting is choosing which long-term projects to back. Projects are independent (judged alone) or mutually exclusive (pick one, reject the rest). Four techniques compete to make the call — two ignore the time value of money, and two respect it.

1 · Payback Period — how fast you get your money back

The time for cumulative cash flows to repay the initial outlay. Quick, intuitive, and favours liquidity — but it ignores the time value of money, ignores everything after the cut-off, and needs an arbitrary benchmark.

2 · Accounting Rate of Return (ARR)

Average annual profit ÷ average investment, as a %. Accept if it beats a target. Easy and ties to ROCE — but it uses accounting profit (not cash), smooths over timing, and again ignores the time value of money. Three projects with identical totals can score the same ARR even though one front-loads its cash.

3 · Net Present Value (NPV) — the gold standard

Discount every cash flow to today and subtract the outlay. NPV > 0 → accept (it adds to shareholder wealth); for mutually exclusive projects, take the highest positive NPV.

NPV = −C0 + Σ Ct ÷ (1 + r)t
C0 the initial outlay · Ct the cash flow in year t · r the discount / hurdle rate.

4 · Internal Rate of Return (IRR)

The discount rate that makes NPV exactly zero — the project's own break-even return. Accept if IRR > the required return. Expressed as a %, so it's easy to compare, but it can mislead on scale and on unusual cash-flow patterns. When NPV and IRR disagree, trust NPV.

⊞ The Reckoner · Project NPV
🏛 Real Story

The UK's HS2 rail line was appraised at around £56bn; estimates later ballooned past £108bn with the opening date repeatedly slipping. A reminder that an appraisal is only as good as its cash-flow forecasts — garbage in, garbage out.

💡 Did You Know?

Netflix's 2007 decision to invest $40m in streaming technology had a negative short-term payback but a transformational NPV. If they'd used payback alone, they'd still be mailing DVDs. The method you choose literally shapes the future of the firm.

🔑 Lock It In

NPV > 0 accept · IRR > hurdle accept · shortest Payback wins. Payback and ARR ignore time; NPV and IRR don't. When two clash, NPV is king because it measures pounds of wealth, not percentages.

💡 Did You Know?

The IRR is just the discount rate where the NPV line crosses zero. Spreadsheet users never solve it by hand — =IRR(cashflows) does it instantly — but the exam still wants the trial-and-error interpolation between two guesses.

Lecture 06 · Investment & Risk

Risk & Return

The big idea You're only rewarded for the risk you can't diversify away — and that surviving slice, market risk, is measured by beta.

Hold a share and your return comes from two streams: dividends and capital gains (the price change). Risk is the chance the actual return differs from what you expected — its volatility, measured by the standard deviation of returns.

Two kinds of risk

  • Unsystematic (firm-specific) risk — a strike, a product flop, a CEO leaving. Diversifiable: hold many shares and these average out.
  • Systematic (market) risk — inflation, interest rates, recessions. Hits everyone at once and cannot be diversified away.

So diversification — not putting all your eggs in one basket — kills firm-specific risk for free. What's left is market risk, and that's the only risk the market pays you to bear.

Beta — measuring market risk

Beta (β) is how much a stock moves when the market moves 1%. β > 1 amplifies the market (M&S ≈ 1.6); β < 1 dampens it (Tesco ≈ 0.58); β = 0 means no market sensitivity. Beta is unitless systematic risk — distinct from standard deviation, which captures total risk.

CAPM — the price of risk

The Capital Asset Pricing Model turns beta into a required return: start from the risk-free rate and add beta times the market's equity risk premium. It gives the minimum return an investor should demand — and the cost of equity used in WACC.

E(ri) = Rf + βi × ( E(rm) − Rf )
Rf risk-free rate · β the asset's beta · ( E(rm) − Rf ) the equity risk premium.
⊞ The Reckoner · CAPM Required Return
🏛 Real Story

The FTSE 100 swung violently through the Global Financial Crisis, COVID-19 and Brexit — proof that market (systematic) risk is real and unavoidable. No amount of diversifying across UK shares would have spared you those market-wide plunges.

💡 Did You Know?

A stock can have a lower beta but higher total volatility than another — that gap is pure firm-specific risk. Tesco's beta sits below M&S's, yet its share price can still be the bumpier ride.

💡 Did You Know?

The equity risk premium has averaged about 5–6% historically in the UK and US, but it varies wildly by decade. In the 1980s it was closer to 8%; in the 2010s, barely 4%. Your CAPM answer is only as good as your premium guess.

🔑 Lock It In

Reward = risk-free + β × market premium. The Security Market Line plots it: above the line the asset is underpriced (great deal), below it overpriced. Only β earns you a premium — firm risk you should have diversified away.

Lecture 07 · Financing the Firm

Sources of Finance

The big idea Every pound a firm raises is either borrowed (debt) or owned (equity) — and that single choice sets its obligations, its risk and who controls it.

The mix of debt and equity a firm uses is its capital structure. Finance comes from internal sources (retained earnings — the CFO's clear favourite) and external ones (new shares, bonds, bank loans). Surveys of CFOs rank the pecking order the same way again and again: retained earnings first, debt second, new equity last.

Debt looks cheap — interest is tax-deductible and must be paid before any dividend — but those compulsory payments add financial risk. Equity never has to be repaid and shares the risk, yet it's the costliest and most public to raise. The tension between them is the whole of capital structure theory.

💡 Did You Know?

The world finances itself differently: bonds dominate corporate debt in the United States, while bank lending still rules in the Euro area and China. Geography shapes the capital structure as much as theory does.

💡 Did You Know?

Apple — despite sitting on $200 billion in cash — still issues bonds. Why? Much of it is trapped overseas, and repatriating it triggers US tax. It's cheaper to borrow at 2% than to pay 35% tax to bring cash home. Even cash-rich firms use debt.

🔑 Lock It In

Pecking order: Retained earnings → Debt → New equity. Cheapest and least hassle first, most expensive and most scrutinised last. Debt = a fixed claim; equity = a residual claim.

🌍 In Plain Life

It's the difference between a bank loan and taking on a business partner. The loan demands fixed repayments whatever happens; the partner wants no fixed payment but a permanent share of the profits — and a say in decisions.

Lecture 08 · Financing the Firm

Capital Structure: Debt

The big idea A bond is just a tradeable loan; its fair price is the present value of its promised cash, and the rate that makes price and cash agree is its yield.

Beyond bank loans and leases, the headline form of debt is the bond — a marketable loan between an issuer (borrower) and investors (lenders). Each bond fixes its terms: a face value (usually £100 or £1,000), a coupon (annual interest as a % of face value), and a maturity date when the face value is repaid.

Pricing a bond = present value

A straight bond's cash flows are an annuity of coupons plus the face value at maturity. Its fair price is simply all of that discounted to today:

Price = Σ C ÷ (1+r)t + F ÷ (1+r)n
C the coupon · F the face value · r the discount rate (the yield) · n years to maturity.

Yield to Maturity — the true return

Turn the equation around: given the market price, the Yield to Maturity (YTM) is the discount rate that makes the present value of the cash equal that price. It's the real return a buyer earns, not the coupon. The YTM stacks up three building blocks:

  • Short-term risk-free rate — inflation plus the real return (the Fisher equation).
  • Maturity premium — extra reward for lending longer; long bonds swing more when rates move.
  • Default premium — extra reward for credit risk, set by ratings (AAA down to junk).

Two cousins: a convertible bond can be swapped for shares (value it at the higher of face value or conversion value), and a zero-coupon bond pays no coupon — all the return comes from buying it deep below face value.

⊞ The Reckoner · Bond Price & Yield
🌍 In Plain Life

A bond is an IOU you can sell on. If you lock in a 4% IOU and new IOUs start paying 6%, nobody will buy yours at full price — so it trades at a discount. That's why bond prices fall when interest rates rise.

💡 Did You Know?

Credit ratings draw a hard line: BBB− and above is "investment grade"; below it is "junk". Many pension funds are legally barred from junk, so a single downgrade across that border can force a wave of selling.

💡 Did You Know?

The longest bond ever issued was a 1,000-year bond by the Canadian Pacific Railway in 1883. It was finally redeemed in 1993 — after 110 years. Talk about long-term finance.

🔑 Lock It In

Coupon > yield → premium (price above par). Coupon < yield → discount. Rates up, price down. Yield is the return that matters; the coupon is just the sticker.

Capital Structure: Equity

The big idea A share is worth the present value of every dividend it will ever pay — which makes the growth assumption the most powerful number in the model.

Equity is ownership. Ordinary shares carry votes and an unfixed dividend, paid last of all. Preference shares skip the vote but take a fixed dividend ahead of ordinary holders. Firms raise fresh equity by retaining earnings, an IPO or placing, or a rights issue to existing owners.

Three values of a share

  • Nominal (par) value — an arbitrary figure on the books, often £1 or less.
  • Book value — net assets (assets − liabilities) per share, from the balance sheet.
  • Market value — what it trades at today; market cap = price × shares.

Valuing a share — the dividend model

Because dividends go on indefinitely, a share is a perpetuity of dividends. If they grow at a constant rate g, the Gordon Growth Model values them in one line:

P0 = D1 ÷ ( re − g )
D1 next year's dividend · re the required return on equity (cost of equity) · g the constant dividend growth rate (must be below re).

Rearranged, it hands you the cost of equity = D1/P0 + g (dividend yield plus growth) — the very number CAPM also estimates and that feeds WACC.

Rights issues & TERP

A rights issue sells new shares cheaply to existing holders. The Theoretical Ex-Rights Price (TERP) is the weighted average of the old price and the issue price. Whether you take up, sell, or lapse the rights, your wealth is unchanged in theory — only lapsing actually loses you money.

⊞ The Reckoner · Gordon Growth Share Value
🔑 Lock It In

P₀ = D₁ ÷ (r − g): next year's dividend over the gap between return and growth. The narrower that gap, the higher — and more fragile — the valuation. g must always be less than r.

💡 Did You Know?

Because the value hinges on the gap (r − g), nudging growth from 3% to 4% when the required return is 5% doesn't raise the price a little — it doubles it. Tiny growth assumptions move giant valuations.

💡 Did You Know?

During the dot-com bubble, analysts justified sky-high prices by assuming growth rates of 15–20% forever. When reality returned, the NASDAQ fell 78%. The Gordon model is powerful — and dangerous when the gap is too tight.

🌍 In Plain Life

An increased dividend is a confident wave from management; a cut is a distress flare. That's why boards loathe cutting dividends even in a bad year — the signal can hurt more than the cash saved.

Cost of Capital & WACC

The big idea A firm's cost of capital is the blended return all its investors demand — and the hurdle every new project must clear to create value.

A firm funded by both debt and equity must satisfy both groups. The Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC) blends the cost of each, weighted by its share of the total — and crucially gives debt a discount for its tax shield.

WACC = (E/V)·re + (D/V)·rd·(1 − T)
E equity, D debt, V = E+D · re cost of equity · rd cost of debt · T the corporate tax rate.

WACC is the discount rate for NPV and the benchmark for IRR. A firm wants it low — a lower hurdle means more projects clear it.

Leverage: the double-edged sword

Financial leverage means using debt to fund investment. Because debt is cheaper than equity, a dash of it lifts the return on equity — but it also magnifies losses and raises the risk of financial distress.

Is there an optimal structure?

Trade-Off Theory: debt's tax shield lowers WACC, but rising bankruptcy risk eventually pushes it back up — so there's a target range that maximises value. Pecking Order Theory: firms don't chase an optimum at all; they just prefer the cheapest source first (retained earnings, then debt, then equity), driven by issue costs and asymmetric information.

⊞ The Reckoner · Leverage & Return on Equity
🏛 Real Story

Franco Modigliani and Merton Miller won Nobel Prizes for showing that, in a perfect market with no taxes, capital structure doesn't change firm value at all. The real world's taxes and bankruptcy costs are exactly what make the mix matter — and what the Trade-Off Theory tries to balance.

🔑 Lock It In

Weight each source by market value; tax-shield the debt. Leverage lifts returns when the project beats the interest rate — and deepens the hole when it doesn't.

💡 Did You Know?

The tax shield is why debt is the only source whose cost you cut for tax. At a 21% tax rate, a 5% loan really costs the firm just 3.95% — the taxman effectively subsidises the borrowing.

💡 Did You Know?

Private equity firms routinely use 60–80% debt (leveraged buyouts) because the tax shield is so powerful. When it works, returns are extraordinary. When it doesn't — as with Toys "R" Us in 2017 — the bankruptcy is swift and brutal.

Lecture 11 · Payout, Hedging & Deals

Dividend Policy

The big idea In a perfect world, how you split earnings between dividends and reinvestment doesn't change firm value — but in the real world it sends powerful signals.

A dividend pays earnings out to owners. Types run from regular and special (one-off) to stock dividends (more shares, not cash). The ex-dividend date is the cut-off: buy before it and you get the next payment; buy on or after, and the seller keeps it.

Does dividend policy matter?

  • M&M irrelevance — Modigliani & Miller again: value comes from investment decisions, not from how earnings are split. Cut the dividend and reinvest well, and value is unharmed.
  • Signalling — in reality, a dividend rise signals confidence and a cut signals trouble; prices react sharply.
  • Bird-in-the-hand & clientele — many investors prefer cash now, and different clienteles (pension funds vs growth investors) want different payout patterns.

Dividends vs share repurchases

A buyback returns cash by repurchasing shares instead of paying a dividend. In a perfect market the shareholder's wealth is identical either way — but buybacks can be more tax-efficient (capital gains vs income) and signal that managers think the shares are cheap. The dividend valuation model and Gordon model both price a share off its expected dividend stream.

🏛 Real Story

Microsoft and Apple spent their early high-growth years paying no dividend at all, reinvesting every pound — and rewarded shareholders through soaring capital gains instead. Proof that "no dividend" can be a perfectly rational policy for a growth firm.

🔑 Lock It In

M&M: value lives in the investments, not the payout. Five policies to know: constant dividend, constant growth, constant payout ratio, residual, and no dividend.

💡 Did You Know?

Share buybacks have surged so much that in the EU they now exceed half the value of ordinary dividends — a quiet shift in how companies hand cash back to their owners.

💡 Did You Know?

In 2024, UK firms returned a record £85 billion to shareholders via dividends and buybacks — more than total UK government spending on defence. The scale of corporate payout is staggering.

Lecture 12 · Payout, Hedging & Deals

Financial Risk Management

The big idea Hedging doesn't delete risk — it trades away uncertainty (and usually the upside too) to protect cash flow, like buying insurance.

Every firm — not just banks — faces volatility in interest rates, exchange rates and commodity prices. Hedging (immunisation) reduces that exposure using derivatives — instruments whose value derives from an underlying asset. Perfect hedging is rare; the goal is risk reduction, not elimination.

The four instruments

  • Forwards — a bespoke, over-the-counter agreement to buy/sell at a fixed price on a future date. No upfront cost; a zero-sum game; settled only at maturity. Carries counterparty (credit) risk.
  • Futures — like forwards but standardised and exchange-traded, with daily marking-to-market that slashes default risk. Enables cross-hedging (e.g. hedging jet fuel with crude oil).
  • Swaps — exchange streams of cash flows; essentially a series of forwards. Interest-rate swaps trade fixed for floating; currency and commodity swaps exist too.
  • Options — the right, not the obligation, to buy (call) or sell (put) at a strike price, for an upfront premium. Uniquely, they cap the downside while keeping the upside.

🏛 Real Story

In 1993 German firm Metallgesellschaft lost over $1 billion hedging long-term oil contracts with short-term futures — a maturity mismatch. The lesson: a badly designed hedge can create risk rather than remove it.

🌍 In Plain Life

A fixed-price energy tariff is a hedge. You give up the chance of cheaper bills if prices fall, in exchange for certainty if they rise. An option is closer to insurance — pay a premium, keep the good outcomes, cap the bad.

💡 Did You Know?

The global derivatives market is estimated at over $600 trillion in notional value — roughly 6× world GDP. Most of this is interest-rate swaps between banks, not speculation. Derivatives are the plumbing of modern finance.

🔑 Lock It In

Forwards bespoke & OTC; Futures standard & daily-settled; Swaps = a string of forwards; Options = a right, not an obligation. Only options let you keep the upside — that's what the premium buys.

Mergers & Acquisitions

The big idea Two firms combine because they should be worth more together than apart — but that synergy is promised far more often than it's delivered.

The vocabulary matters. A merger absorbs one firm into another (the acquirer survives). A consolidation dissolves both into a brand-new entity. An acquisition (takeover) buys another firm's shares or assets — and can be friendly (management cooperates) or hostile (a direct tender offer to shareholders over management's objections).

Three types of acquisition

  • Horizontal — a rival in the same industry (market share, scale, less competition).
  • Vertical — a firm up or down the supply chain (control costs, secure supply).
  • Conglomerate — an unrelated business (diversification, spreading risk).

Why do it — and how to pay

Motives: synergy, faster growth, diversification, economies of scale, tax losses — and sometimes plain managerial ego. The acquirer forecasts the target's cash flows, discounts them, and offers a price above market. Pay in cash, shares, or debt — but overpay, and you simply transfer value from your shareholders to the target's. Targets resist with defensive tactics: poison pills, golden parachutes, white knights, greenmail.

🏛 Real Story

Vodafone's $183bn takeover of Mannesmann (2000) is still the largest completed acquisition in history. That same year, AOL / Time Warner ($165bn) became the textbook case of synergy failure — a merger that destroyed staggering value. Bigger is not always better.

💡 Did You Know?

M&A keeps breaking records: the 2025 $55bn Silver Lake-led buyout of Electronic Arts became the largest leveraged buyout ever, and Rio Tinto / Glencore talks in 2026 could create a $260bn mining giant.

💡 Did You Know?

Studies show 70–90% of mergers fail to create value for the acquirer's shareholders. The winner is almost always the target's shareholders, who pocket the premium. M&A is a transfer of wealth, not a creation of it.

🔑 Lock It In

Horizontal = rivals · Vertical = supply chain · Conglomerate = strangers. Value is created only if synergy exceeds the premium paid — discipline at the negotiating table matters more than the deal's size.
